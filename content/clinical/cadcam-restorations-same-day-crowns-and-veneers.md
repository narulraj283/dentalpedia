## Clinical Overview of Same-Day Restoration Technology

Same-day CAD/CAM restorations represent a paradigm shift in esthetic prosthodontics, delivering complete crown and veneer fabrication during a single clinical appointment. This single-visit approach requires intraoral optical scanning, digital design, chairside milling, and cementation—typically completed within 45-90 minutes for crown restorations and 30-60 minutes for veneer preparations. Clinical evidence demonstrates that same-day restorations achieve marginal discrepancies of 40-60 micrometers, comparable to or superior to laboratory-fabricated alternatives, with five-year success rates of 94-98% depending on material selection. The elimination of temporary restorations reduces postoperative sensitivity by 70-80% and eliminates bacterial contamination risks inherent to multi-visit protocols.

## Indications for Same-Day Crown Restorations

Clinical candidates for same-day crowns include single-tooth restorations on vital or endodontically treated teeth with adequate remaining structure. Optimal preparation geometry features 6-12 degree axial wall divergence, rounded internal line angles with 1.0-1.5 mm radius, and 1.2-1.5 mm occlusal/incisal reduction. Posterior teeth demonstrate highest success rates due to favorable stress distribution and reduced esthetic demands; failure rates of 1-2% at five years reflect primarily restoration fracture rather than biological complications. Anterior teeth require additional consideration of esthetic variables including tooth shade (30+ shade references available in contemporary systems), translucency requirements, and surface texture specifications. Single-tooth restorations avoid the biological and mechanical complications associated with multi-unit bridge designs, supporting 96-98% five-year success rates.

## Veneer Preparation and Design Parameters

Same-day veneer fabrication addresses anterior esthetic concerns with minimal tooth structure removal—typically 0.3-0.5 mm facial reduction, 0.2-0.3 mm interproximal shaving, and lingual reduction limited to cingulum areas to preserve pulpal vitality. Digital design algorithms calculate veneer thickness (0.6-1.0 mm for anterior teeth) to optimize strength while maintaining natural translucency. Incisal designs employ two primary strategies: overlapping veneers extending 0.5-1.0 mm over incisal edges reduce fracture risk but sacrifice natural edge characterization, while incisal cuttings that preserve native incisal geometry improve esthetics at the cost of slightly increased fracture vulnerability. Clinical follow-up data spanning eight years demonstrates 91-94% success rates for veneer restorations, with primary failure modes involving adhesive debonding (3-4%) rather than material fracture (2-3%).

## Digital Scanning Protocols for Esthetic Precision

Intraoral scanning for esthetic restorations requires meticulous technique to capture facial contours, embrasure geometry, and interproximal anatomy. Advanced optical systems generate point clouds of 5-10 million data points, with individual point accuracy of ±15 micrometer—sufficient for capturing subtle anatomic details. Scanning protocols typically initiate with occlusal surface capture (5-8 seconds per tooth), followed by facial scanning from multiple angulations (10-15 seconds), interproximal zones (5-8 seconds per aspect), and lingual surfaces (3-5 seconds). Digital smile design software overlays scanned tooth preparations onto full-face photographs, enabling virtual esthetic modifications before milling. This digital approach reduces esthetic complications by 60-70% compared to conventional impression methods, particularly for multi-tooth cases requiring interdental balance and emergence profile optimization.

## Material Selection for Esthetic Restorations

Lithium disilicate glass-ceramic represents optimal material for anterior same-day crowns and veneers, combining 350-400 MPa fracture strength with superior optical properties including natural translucency gradients. This material demonstrates 96-98% five-year survival rates in anterior applications, with minimal chipping (0.5-1.0%) and excellent marginal integrity. Polymer-infiltrated ceramic networks (PICN) provide intermediate properties between ceramic and resin composite, with 350-400 MPa strength and more conservative preparation geometries—beneficial for teeth with minimal coronal structure. These materials show 93-95% five-year success rates. Monolithic zirconia, while optimal for posterior applications, produces optical opacity limiting anterior esthetic applications; however, high-translucent zirconia variants (800+ MPa) have emerged with 88-92% anterior five-year success rates, expanding indications for parafunctional patients. Material selection incorporates 12-15% volume expansion compensation for sintering processes, calculated by software and compensated in milling tool paths.

## Cementation Protocols and Adhesive Considerations

Same-day restorations employ resin-modified glass ionomer (RMGI) or dual-cure resin cements depending on clinical circumstances. RMGI cements (strength 100-150 MPa) are advantageous for conservative preparations and veneers where tooth structure is well-preserved and minimal retention is required; they demonstrate excellent biocompatibility and provide fluoride release for secondary caries prevention. Dual-cure resin cements (200-300 MPa) offer superior bond strength and marginal seal, particularly for endodontically treated teeth or deeply retentive crown preparations. Etch-and-rinse bonding protocols (generating 30-40 MPa dentin bond strength) are preferred over self-etch systems for optimal adhesion to freshly cut dentin. Preparation moisture control is critical; excessive wetness reduces bond strength by 25-30%, while excessive desiccation causes dentin dehydration and bond degradation. Clinical technique employs 37% phosphoric acid for 15-20 seconds, gentle air drying to remove excess water while maintaining dentin surface moisture, followed by bonding resin light-cured for 10-15 seconds at 500 mW/cm² intensity.

## Occlusal Adjustment Technique and Verification

CAD/CAM milling systems simulate dynamic occlusal contacts through proprietary articulation software, but clinical verification remains essential. Articulating paper (40 micrometer thickness) applied with 5-7 kg pressure identifies initial contacts; most restorations demonstrate 2-3 contact points requiring selective reduction. Cuspal and incisocervical areas typically generate first contact, requiring careful grinding to avoid creating planar contacts. Clinical technique employs fine-grit diamond points (#15 micrometer for finishing) to reduce contact points to 15-20 micrometer thickness. Lateral excursive movements (6-10 mm width) should demonstrate point contacts on facial inclines during laterotrusion, without simultaneous opposing tooth contact—critical for protecting lateral restorations from cantilever loading. Verification employs 12 micrometer floss passage—floss should pass interproximal contacts with perceptible resistance but without tissue blanching. Final adjustments achieve 5-8 minute average adjustment time per crown, significantly less than conventional laboratory-fabricated restorations requiring 20-30 minutes adjustment.

## Clinical Considerations for Multiple Adjacent Restorations

Multi-tooth same-day restorations (2-3 adjacent teeth) present increased complexity due to interdental embrasure balance, emergence profile harmony, and collective occlusal force distribution. Digital design software calculates contact point positioning (typically 21-25 mm apical to embrasure tip for anterior teeth) with 0.5-1.0 mm spacing adjustment for optimal embrasure form. Emergence profiles are designed with 0.5-1.0 mm convergence angle, preventing unnatural "bubble" contours while maintaining cleansability. Occlusal surface planes are calibrated using reference geometry from unrestored teeth, ensuring restored and native teeth achieve parallel plane relationships with less than 1 mm vertical discrepancy. Clinical outcomes for 2-3 tooth restorations demonstrate 94-96% five-year success rates, slightly lower than single-tooth cases due to increased mechanical loading and more complex technical demands.

## Postoperative Management and Patient Longevity

Patients receive detailed postoperative instructions including 24-hour avoidance of hard/sticky foods (allowing cement polymerization to complete), standard oral hygiene reinforcement, and monitoring for delayed sensitivity. Sensitivity incidence ranges from 3-8% of cases, typically resolving within 2-4 weeks as dentin tubule occlusion occurs; persistent sensitivity warrants evaluation for marginal leakage or excessive preparation depth. Recall intervals follow 6-12 month schedules for the first year, then annual examinations for long-term monitoring. Clinical data demonstrates that 6-8 year longevity correlates directly with marginal adaptation quality (restorations with ≤50 micrometer gaps show 98% retention compared to 85% with >100 micrometer gaps) and patient oral hygiene compliance. Parafunctional patients (bruxism, clenching) demonstrate 8-12% higher failure rates and require protective night guards to extend restoration longevity to 10-12 year intervals.

## Clinical Cost-Effectiveness and Practice Implementation

Same-day restorations deliver superior economics compared to conventional laboratory fabrication: eliminated laboratory fees ($150-300 per unit), eliminated temporary restorations, consolidated patient visits, and reduced chair time for adjustment and delivery. Direct material costs range $60-120 per unit, compared to total conventional costs of $300-500 including laboratory, temporary, and adjustment time. Practice implementation requires $85,000-150,000 initial investment (intraoral scanner, milling unit, software), recovered through efficiency gains within 18-24 months. Patient acceptance of same-day completion exceeds 85-90% across demographic groups, with particular enthusiasm among working patients who value single-visit completion. Practice surveys report average revenue increase of 30-40% following CAD/CAM integration, attributed to increased patient referrals and expanded restorative case acceptance.

## Summary

Same-day CAD/CAM restorations represent contemporary standard of care for single and limited multi-tooth esthetic and functional restoration. Digital scanning protocols capture tooth geometry with 15-30 micrometer accuracy, while milling systems deliver marginal adaptation of 40-60 micrometers—superior to conventional laboratory alternatives. Lithium disilicate and PICN materials provide esthetic excellence with 94-98% five-year survival rates in anterior applications, while monolithic zirconia assures posterior durability. Single-visit completion eliminates temporary restorations, reduces postoperative sensitivity, and improves patient satisfaction. Evidence-based occlusal adjustment protocols, etch-and-rinse adhesive bonding, and meticulous cementation technique ensure restorations achieve longevity of 10-15 years. Integration of same-day restoration technology enhances clinical efficiency, patient outcomes, and practice profitability.
