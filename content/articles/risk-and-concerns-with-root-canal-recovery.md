## What to Expect After Your Root Canal Treatment

You've just completed root canal treatment—a procedure that saves your tooth by removing infected nerve tissue. You might feel some discomfort or sensitivity afterward, but understanding what's normal helps you know whether you need additional care. Most root canals heal smoothly with proper aftercare.

## Normal Pain After Root Canal Treatment

For more on what to expect, see our guide on [root canal treatment](/articles/root-canal-treatment-complete-guide/). Some pain or discomfort after root canal treatment is expected. Your tooth and surrounding tissues have been through a procedure, and they need time to settle. For the first few days, your tooth might feel sensitive to touch or pressure, especially when chewing. This usually goes away within a week.

You might also experience a mild ache in the tooth or surrounding area. Over-the-counter pain medication like ibuprofen or acetaminophen typically controls this discomfort effectively. Pain that's manageable with medication and improves gradually over a few days is normal.

## When Pain Is a Problem

Severe pain that doesn't improve with medication, or pain that gets worse instead of better after a few days, suggests something might be wrong. Contact your dentist if you have extreme pain, pain shooting up into your sinuses, or pain that radiates into your jaw or ear. These symptoms might indicate infection, inflammation that needs treatment, or other complications.

The difference between normal and problematic pain matters. Normal pain is manageable, gradually improving, and doesn't prevent you from functioning. Problematic pain is severe, stays the same or gets worse, and interferes with eating and sleeping.

## Swelling and Inflammation

Mild swelling around the treated tooth is possible after root canal treatment. This usually peaks within the first day or two and gradually subsides over a few days to a week. Ice packs applied for 15 minutes can help reduce swelling.

However, swelling that's significant, that gets worse after improving initially, or that doesn't improve after a few days needs professional evaluation. Increasing swelling suggests ongoing inflammation or infection needing treatment.

## Sensitivity to Temperature

After root canal treatment, your tooth might feel sensitive to hot or cold. This usually fades within a few weeks as inflammation reduces and your tooth settles. Sensitivity that gradually improves is normal. If sensitivity gets worse or persists beyond a few weeks, tell your dentist.

## When Your Bite Feels Off

Sometimes after root canal treatment, your tooth might feel like it's hitting harder than other teeth when you bite down. This is common and usually resolves as swelling decreases. Be careful not to chew hard on the treated tooth while it's healing—favor the other side of your mouth for a few days if possible.

If the bite feeling wrong persists beyond a few days, tell your dentist. Your dentist can adjust your bite if needed, relieving pressure on the healing tooth.

## Infection After Root Canal

True infection after root canal treatment is uncommon but possible. Signs include fever, significant and increasing swelling (especially if your face becomes puffy), pus or bad taste/odor coming from around the tooth, or severe pain despite medication. These symptoms require immediate professional attention.

If you develop signs of infection, contact your dentist right away. Don't wait—infection can spread and become serious. Your dentist will assess whether additional treatment is needed.

## Crown Placement Timing

Understanding the importance of [tooth restoration options](/articles/risk-and-concerns-with-tooth-restoration-comparison/) helps protect your investment. Your dentist likely recommends a permanent crown on your root canal-treated tooth within a few weeks. This crown protects the treated tooth from breaking (treated teeth become more brittle) and seals the access opening. Don't delay getting your crown—teeth without crowns after root canal have higher failure rates.

## Healing Timeline

Most post-root canal discomfort resolves within 1 to 2 weeks. Your tooth might still be slightly sensitive when chewing for several weeks, but severe symptoms should be gone. If discomfort continues beyond 2 weeks or gets worse instead of better, tell your dentist.

Complete internal healing of bone around your tooth root takes much longer—months to years. But from your perspective, your tooth should feel normal within a few weeks.

## Activities and Restrictions

Avoid chewing on the treated tooth for several days or until your crown is placed. Stick to soft foods on the other side of your mouth. Avoid very hot foods and beverages while your tooth is sensitive. Normal activities like work and school are fine—you don't need bed rest after root canal treatment.

## Medication After Root Canal

Your dentist might prescribe antibiotics if infection was present. Take these exactly as directed, even if you feel better. Finish the entire course. Also take any prescribed or recommended pain medication as needed.

## Root Canal Failure: Rare but Possible

Most root canals succeed—studies show success rates around 85 to 95 percent. But some root canals don't work out, either because the infection wasn't completely removed or because new infection develops later. If your tooth continues hurting months later, or if a problem develops years after treatment, you might need root canal retreatment or extraction.

This is why following your dentist's instructions and getting your crown placed promptly matters—these steps protect your treatment success.

## Conclusion

Post-root canal discomfort is normal and manageable for most people. Pain that's mild to moderate, gradually improving, and controllable with medication is expected. Severe pain, signs of infection, or lack of improvement after a few days warrant professional evaluation. Following your dentist's instructions, avoiding chewing on the treated tooth, and getting your crown placed as recommended support successful healing.

## Key Takeaway

Most root canal treatment recovery is straightforward. Expect mild pain and sensitivity for a few days to a week, manageable with over-the-counter pain medication. Normal healing means pain gradually improves daily. Warning signs needing immediate attention include severe pain, swelling that worsens, fever, pus, or signs of infection. Contact your dentist immediately if you develop these symptoms.
