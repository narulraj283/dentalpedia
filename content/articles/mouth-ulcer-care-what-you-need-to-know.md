Mouth ulcers affect approximately 20% of the general population at some point in their lives, making them one of the most common oral conditions dentists encounter in clinical practice. While most ulcers are self-limiting and resolve within 7-14 days, patient distress and functional impairment warrant prompt clinical intervention. Understanding ulcer classification, etiology, and evidence-based treatment protocols enables dentists to provide appropriate care and reassurance while managing patient expectations regarding healing timelines and symptom resolution.

## Classification and Clinical Presentation of Oral Ulcers

Oral ulcers present in diverse morphologies and require systematic classification to guide treatment decisions. The three primary categories of recurrent aphthous stomatitis (RAS)—minor, major, and herpetiform—account for approximately 80% of ulcer presentations. Minor aphthous ulcers, representing 80% of RAS cases, measure less than 1 cm in diameter, present with erythematous halos, and typically cause moderate discomfort. These lesions appear most frequently on the buccal mucosa, alveolar ridge, dorsum of tongue, and floor of mouth—areas subjected to mechanical trauma during mastication. Major aphthous ulcers exceed 1 cm in diameter, penetrate deeper into the lamina propria, generate severe pain disproportionate to their clinical appearance, and can persist for 3-6 weeks, frequently leaving scarring upon resolution.

Herpetiform aphthous ulcers present as clusters of 10-100 small ulcerations (0.5-1 mm diameter) that often coalesce into larger lesions. These presentations are clinically challenging because the diffuse ulceration creates widespread oral discomfort and may compromise adequate oral hygiene compliance during healing phases. Traumatic ulcers, distinct from RAS, result from direct mechanical injury and generally heal within 1-2 weeks following trauma cessation. Chemical ulcers from caustic substances demonstrate rapid onset following exposure and require immediate intervention to neutralize the causative agent. Understanding these distinctions prevents inappropriate treatment protocols and manages patient expectations regarding healing trajectories. Minor ulcers typically epithelialize within 7-14 days without intervention, while major ulcers may require 3-4 weeks even with aggressive treatment, and herpetiform presentations may take 2-3 weeks despite topical and systemic interventions.

## Etiology and Contributing Factors

Multiple etiologic pathways converge to generate recurrent aphthous stomatitis. Viral triggers including herpes simplex virus (HSV), Epstein-Barr virus, and human papillomavirus have been implicated in RAS pathogenesis, though the exact mechanistic relationships remain incompletely understood. Nutritional deficiencies represent a significant modifiable risk factor; deficiency of vitamin B12, folate, iron, and zinc substantially increases RAS susceptibility. Studies demonstrate that approximately 27-60% of patients with recurrent aphthous ulcers present with at least one nutritional deficiency, making screening for these micronutrients essential in patients with frequent ulceration. Serum ferritin levels below 15 ng/mL, vitamin B12 less than 200 pg/mL, or folate below 2.7 ng/mL warrant supplementation protocols.

Aphthous ulceration correlates strongly with trauma from dental hardware, sharp food edges, or aggressive oral hygiene practices. Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) in conventional dentifrices may contribute to ulcer initiation in susceptible individuals by disrupting the mucous membrane protective barrier. Psychological stress remains a well-documented trigger; approximately 40% of RAS patients report temporal clustering of ulcers with periods of high psychological stress. Immunologic dysregulation, particularly deficiencies in CD4+ T-cell populations, predisposes to more severe and frequent RAS presentations. Oral mucosal contact allergens—including cinnamon flavoring, mint extracts, and propylene glycol in oral care products—can precipitate delayed-type hypersensitivity reactions manifesting as recurrent ulceration. Tobacco, alcohol, and spicy foods may exacerbate existing ulcers but do not represent primary etiologic agents in most cases. Systemic conditions including celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis), Behçet's syndrome, and immunodeficiency states produce chronic or frequently recurrent ulceration requiring systemic evaluation and management.

## Topical Treatment Protocols for Symptomatic Relief

Topical corticosteroid application represents first-line management for both minor and major aphthous ulcers, with triamcinolone acetonide 0.1% demonstrating superior efficacy to placebo in reducing ulcer duration and pain severity. Clinical evidence supports the application of triamcinolone acetonide as an oral paste (Kenalog in Orabase) directly onto the ulcer three to four times daily, with optimal application performed immediately after meals and before bedtime. The paste provides mucoadhesive delivery, allowing steroid penetration into the ulcer bed for extended periods. An alternative delivery method involves using a steroid-impregnated gauze pack applied directly to the ulcer for 15-20 minutes twice daily, particularly effective for larger major ulcers. Fluocinonide 0.05% gel represents another effective topical steroid option, applied directly to the ulcer surface twice daily; studies demonstrate that fluocinonide produces pain reduction within 2-3 days and complete ulcer epithelialization within 5-7 days for minor ulcers.

Non-steroid topical agents provide adjunctive benefit through distinct mechanisms. Amlexanox paste 5% applied directly to the ulcer four times daily has demonstrated capacity to reduce both healing time and ulcer discomfort, with mechanism of action involving suppression of inflammatory mediator release and enhancement of wound healing. Silver diamine fluoride (SDF) 38% solution applied topically twice weekly provides antimicrobial protection while promoting tissue repair; this agent proves particularly valuable in immunocompromised patients prone to secondary bacterial infection. Benzydamine hydrochloride 0.15% rinse used 2-3 times daily provides local anesthetic and anti-inflammatory effects through COX inhibition, reducing pain without steroid systemic absorption. Topical analgesics including viscous lidocaine 2% applied three to four times daily provide temporary pain relief lasting 15-30 minutes, enabling patients to consume adequate nutrition during the acute ulcer phase. However, systemic absorption of topical lidocaine requires attention in patients applying large quantities multiple times daily, particularly in elderly patients or those with renal compromise.

## Systemic Management of Severe and Recurrent Ulceration

Patients presenting with major aphthous ulcers, herpetiform presentations, or frequent minor ulcers (more than four episodes annually) warrant consideration of systemic therapy. Corticosteroid systemic administration provides dramatic ulcer suppression in severe cases, though prolonged systemic steroid use requires careful patient selection and monitoring protocols. Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day initiated at ulcer onset and tapered over 7-10 days produces rapid pain resolution and accelerated epithelialization, typically generating visible improvement within 3-5 days. However, systemic corticosteroid use should be reserved for severe herpetiform presentations or major ulcers causing significant functional impairment, as adverse effects including immunosuppression, metabolic effects, and adrenal suppression limit long-term application.

For patients with frequent recurrent episodes, colchicine 0.5-1.5 mg daily provides prophylactic benefit through suppression of neutrophil chemotaxis and inflammatory mediator release. Clinical trials demonstrate that colchicine reduces RAS episode frequency by 50-75% with response typically evident after 2-3 weeks of continuous therapy. However, gastrointestinal side effects (diarrhea, abdominal discomfort) limit tolerability in approximately 20% of patients. Pentoxifylline 400 mg three times daily, a rheologic modifier enhancing microcirculation and reducing inflammatory cytokine production, shows promise in open-label studies with approximately 60% of patients experiencing significant reduction in ulcer frequency and severity. Thalidomide represents an option for severe or refractory ulceration, particularly in immunocompromised patients, with typical dosing of 100-300 mg daily producing resolution in major and herpetiform presentations within 2-4 weeks; however, teratogenic risks and need for REMS enrollment limit widespread use. Topical application of intralesional corticosteroid injection (triamcinolone 10 mg/mL) directly into major ulcers provides concentrated anti-inflammatory effect, with injections performed 2-3 times at weekly intervals demonstrating superior efficacy to topical application alone.

## Nutritional Supplementation and Dietary Modification

Comprehensive nutritional assessment should precede aggressive systemic therapy. Patients with documented vitamin B12 deficiency may benefit from sublingual methylcobalamin 1000-2000 mcg daily or intramuscular cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg monthly, with oral supplementation generally taking 2-3 months to restore serum B12 levels. Folate supplementation of 1-5 mg daily addresses deficiency-related ulceration, particularly in patients with malabsorption syndromes. Iron supplementation using ferrous sulfate 325 mg daily (providing 65 mg elemental iron) or ferrous gluconate 325 mg twice daily corrects iron deficiency anemia contributing to RAS; however, iron supplementation requires 2-3 months to normalize serum ferritin and may take an additional 1-2 months to reduce RAS frequency. Zinc supplementation at 15-30 mg daily addresses deficiency states, with particular benefit in patients over 60 years where zinc malabsorption increases ulcer susceptibility.

Dietary modification reduces ulcer triggering and accelerates healing. Elimination of sodium lauryl sulfate-containing dentifrices and substitution with SLS-free formulations reduces RAS frequency by 30-50% in sensitive individuals within 4-6 weeks. Avoidance of cinnamon-flavored products, mint-heavy candies, citric acid-containing foods, and spicy preparations during the acute ulcer phase and during healing windows reduces trigger-related exacerbations. Nutritional support with adequate calories, protein, and micronutrients accelerates epithelialization; oral nutritional supplements (Ensure, Resource) provide concentrated calories and micronutrients when mechanical difficulty consuming solid foods limits adequate intake. Patients should avoid using toothpicks, sharp foods (chips, crusty bread), and aggressive oral hygiene techniques during healing phases.

## Secondary Bacterial Infection Prevention and Management

While primary aphthous ulceration results from viral or immune mechanisms, secondary bacterial superinfection complicates approximately 10-15% of ulcers, particularly major ulcers in immunocompromised patients. Chlorhexidine gluconate 0.12% rinse used twice daily provides broad-spectrum antimicrobial coverage without systemic absorption, reducing bacterial colonization of the ulcer base. Minocycline rinse (prepared as oral rinse from 50 mg minocycline capsule contents dissolved in 30 mL water, used as swish-and-spit twice daily) provides both antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory benefits through tetracycline family mechanisms; however, oral discoloration risks limit extended use. Silver-containing products including colloidal silver rinses or silver diamine fluoride application provide antimicrobial protection while avoiding systemic antibiotic exposure that might drive antimicrobial resistance.

Secondary bacterial infection should be suspected in ulcers showing increased erythema beyond the typical inflammation halo, purulent drainage, lymphadenopathy, or systemic symptoms including fever or malaise. Bacterial culture and sensitivity testing guide antibiotic selection when clinical indicators suggest infection; empiric broad-spectrum coverage using amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125 mg three times daily for 7 days or clindamycin 300 mg three times daily addresses oral Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus species, and anaerobic flora involved in secondary infections. Ulcers demonstrating signs of infection require more aggressive topical therapy and systemic evaluation for immunodeficiency.

## Clinical Evaluation and When to Seek Specialist Consultation

Dentists should maintain a systematic clinical approach to oral ulceration incorporating detailed history, lesion characterization, and appropriate investigation. Lesion duration exceeding 3 weeks warrants direct visualization to exclude malignancy, particularly in patients over 40 years or those with tobacco/alcohol history. Ulcers presenting with rolled or indurated margins, bleeding, or areas of necrotic tissue require immediate biopsy to exclude squamous cell carcinoma or other malignant transformation. The presence of multiple concurrent ulcers (more than five simultaneously) suggests systemic disease including Behçet's syndrome, celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or immunodeficiency, warranting referral to oral medicine or relevant systemic specialist.

Referral to oral medicine specialists should be considered in cases of: frequent major ulcers (more than four annually), herpetiform presentations unresponsive to topical therapy after 2-3 weeks, ulcers persisting beyond 6 weeks despite appropriate treatment, systemic symptoms accompanying ulceration, or ulcers arising in immunocompromised patients. Immunocompromised populations including HIV-positive patients with CD4+ counts below 200, recipients of immunosuppressive therapy, or patients with documented immunoglobulin deficiencies require specialist evaluation given the potential for severe recurrent ulceration and secondary infection risks.

## Long-Term Management and Prevention Strategies

Patients with documented RAS predisposition benefit from personalized prevention protocols addressing modifiable risk factors. Identification and elimination of specific triggers—whether mechanical (poorly fitting dentures, sharp food particles), chemical (caustic oral products), or dietary (acidic beverages, spicy foods)—substantially reduces recurrence frequency. Use of a soft-bristled toothbrush with gentle horizontal brushing technique, floss with careful interproximal technique, and water irrigation devices reduces trauma-related ulceration. Regular dental evaluations identifying and correcting sharp tooth edges, overhanging restorations, or other mechanical irritants provide immediate ulcer prevention benefit. Documentation of RAS episodes in patient records—including location, size, triggering events, symptom severity, and response to treatments—generates longitudinal data informing personalized management algorithms for future episodes.

Patients with significant RAS burden should maintain serum B12, folate, iron, and zinc levels within optimal ranges through routine laboratory assessment and appropriate supplementation, with repeat testing at 3-6 month intervals for patients on supplementation protocols. Stress management techniques including mindfulness-based stress reduction, regular physical activity, and adequate sleep potentially reduce stress-triggered RAS episodes, with typical response timelines of 8-12 weeks. Psychological consultation may benefit patients with anxiety-driven RAS clustering. Patients with suspected systemic diseases (celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, Behçet's syndrome) should be referred for appropriate diagnostic evaluation, as treatment of underlying systemic conditions often results in substantial RAS improvement or resolution.

## Prognosis and Healing Timeline Expectations

The natural history of untreated RAS demonstrates complete epithelialization in approximately 7-14 days for minor ulcers and 14-28 days for major ulcers, with pain typically resolving 2-3 days before visible epithelialization completes. Topical corticosteroid application accelerates healing by 2-4 days while substantially reducing pain severity; patients should expect subjective pain improvement within 24-48 hours of starting triamcinolone or fluocinonide therapy. Systemic corticosteroid administration produces more dramatic effects in major ulcers, with healing completing 7-10 days faster than topical therapy alone and pain resolution typically occurring within 48-72 hours. Herpetiform ulcers respond more slowly to topical therapy, generally requiring 2-3 weeks to complete epithelialization even with aggressive treatment; systemic therapy produces superior outcomes in these presentations.

Patient education regarding realistic healing timelines, appropriate oral hygiene maintenance during healing phases, and dietary modifications optimizes treatment compliance and satisfaction. Patients should be counseled that topical products provide pain relief and may accelerate healing but cannot eliminate healing times entirely; expectations of ulcer resolution within 2-3 days following treatment initiation set unrealistic standards and diminish perceived treatment efficacy. Regular follow-up assessment—particularly for ulcers showing inadequate response after 2-3 weeks of therapy or demonstrating unusual characteristics—ensures appropriate escalation to systemic therapy or specialist consultation when indicated. With systematic clinical assessment, appropriate topical and systemic interventions, and patient education, the vast majority of oral ulcers achieve resolution with minimal long-term morbidity.
