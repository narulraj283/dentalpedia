## Introduction

Oral surgical procedures carry inherent risks of complications that every patient should understand before undergoing treatment. While most oral surgery patients experience uncomplicated healing, some experience difficulties ranging from minor discomfort to significant complications requiring additional treatment. This guide explains the difference between normal postoperative symptoms and signs of complication, describes warning signs that warrant immediate contact with your surgeon, and provides practical strategies for minimizing risks and supporting healing.

## What Is Normal After Oral Surgery?

**Pain and Discomfort** are expected after oral surgery, particularly in the first 3-5 days. Most patients require pain medication only for the first 24-48 hours; pain gradually decreases over the following week. The pattern of pain typically follows a predictable course: maximum pain within the first 24 hours, significant improvement by day 3, and substantial resolution by day 7. If pain is not decreasing by day 3 or suddenly worsens after initial improvement, contact your surgeon immediately.

**Swelling (Edema)** peaks at 24-48 hours post-operatively, which is normal and indicates the body's inflammatory response is working appropriately. Swelling should gradually decrease over the following 7-10 days. Apply ice packs for the first 24 hours (20 minutes on, 20 minutes off) to reduce initial swelling; heat application after 48 hours (warm compresses, 15-20 minute intervals) promotes circulation and reduces persistent swelling. Swelling that worsens after day 3 or remains unchanged after 7 days may indicate infection and should be evaluated.

**Bruising (Ecchymosis)** appears as dark purple/blue discoloration of the skin in the surgical area or adjacent regions, peaking at 48-72 hours and gradually fading over 2-3 weeks. Bruising indicates normal vascular response to tissue trauma and does not indicate complications. Arnica supplements (500-1000 mg orally twice daily) may modestly reduce bruising severity, though evidence is limited.

**Bleeding and Oozing** after surgery is common and typically resolves with gentle pressure application. Some patients notice pink saliva (mixture of blood and saliva) for 24-48 hours; this is normal. Slight oozing when rinsing is expected. However, brisk bleeding (filling the mouth with blood quickly, requiring frequent gauze changes lasting >6 hours) warrants immediate contact with your surgeon.

**Trismus (Reduced Mouth Opening)** frequently occurs, peaking at 24-48 hours post-operatively, with maximum interincisal opening potentially decreasing by 10-15 mm. This reflects normal swelling of the jaw muscles and typically resolves over 7-14 days. Gentle opening exercises (opening the mouth slowly and holding for 5 seconds, repeating 6-8 times per hour) promote recovery of normal function more quickly than immobilization.

**Altered Sensation** in the lip, chin, or tongue is common after wisdom tooth extraction or other procedures near major nerve structures. Temporary numbness typically resolves within 2-8 weeks as nerve conduction recovers. Permanent altered sensation is uncommon (0.5-2% of patients) but should be discussed preoperatively during informed consent. Contact your surgeon if sensation does not show gradual improvement after 8 weeks.

## Signs of Infection—When to Call Your Surgeon

Contact your surgeon immediately if you develop any of the following signs:

**Fever** (body temperature >101.5°F or >38.6°C) developing 24-72 hours post-operatively indicates infection. Measure temperature orally (not in the mouth near the surgical site) using a standard thermometer. Most postoperative patients have temperature elevations of 0.5-1°F due to normal inflammation; fever above this range warrants evaluation. Fever persisting >24 hours or associated with other infection signs (severe swelling, purulent drainage) requires immediate contact.

**Worsening Swelling** after the initial 48-72 hour peak, or localized swelling that appears as a firm mass or "lump" in the surgical area, may indicate abscess formation (pus collection). This typically develops 3-5 days post-operatively and presents as swelling that is unresponsive to heat/ice therapy and often firm to palpation.

**Purulent Drainage or Foul Taste/Odor** indicates bacterial infection with pus formation. Yellow or greenish drainage from the surgical site, or a distinctly foul taste (beyond the normal metallic taste of postoperative healing), warrants immediate evaluation. Swish with warm saltwater (1/2 teaspoon salt in 8 oz water) several times daily; contact your surgeon if drainage persists.

**Difficulty Swallowing (Dysphagia)** or Difficulty Breathing** developing post-operatively should be treated as an emergency. While some difficulty swallowing is normal due to swelling, progressive difficulty or development of stridor (audible breathing noise) suggests airway compromise and requires immediate emergency evaluation. Go to the nearest emergency room if you experience these symptoms.

**Spreading Facial Swelling** extending beyond the immediate surgical site (involving the face, cheek, or neck regions away from the surgical area) indicates spreading cellulitis (tissue infection) and requires prompt antibiotic therapy. This typically develops 48-72 hours post-operatively and warrants same-day evaluation.

**Mouth Opening Unable to Open More Than 1-2 mm** developing >5 days post-operatively, when opening should be gradually improving, indicates severe trismus and possible deeper infection or hematoma. This warrants evaluation to rule out serious complications.

## Pain Management Strategies

**Medication options** for postoperative pain typically include: (1) NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600-800 mg every 6-8 hours, or naproxen 500 mg twice daily), which are most effective for surgical pain and inflammation; (2) acetaminophen (500-1000 mg every 4-6 hours, maximum 3000-4000 mg daily) for patients unable to take NSAIDs; and (3) opioid medications (typically prescribed for 24-48 hours) for severe pain unresponsive to NSAIDs. Do not exceed prescribed opioid doses; these medications carry addiction risk and should be used minimally.

**Non-medication strategies** include: ice application (first 24-48 hours) to numb tissue and reduce swelling, heat application (after 48 hours) to promote circulation, elevation of the head on 2-3 pillows to reduce swelling, and soft diet (avoiding chewing near the surgical site, choosing smooth foods like yogurt, smoothies, applesauce).

**Avoid** alcohol while taking opioid medications (increased risk of overdose and respiratory depression), smoke and tobacco use (delays healing, increases infection risk, increases dry socket risk), and drinking through straws (suction can dislodge blood clot and cause dry socket). Avoid vigorous rinsing or touching the surgical area for 24 hours (can disrupt healing blood clot).

## Dry Socket (Alveolar Osteitis)

Dry socket is a specific complication occurring in 2-5% of routine extractions and up to 20-30% of wisdom tooth extractions. Symptoms develop 2-4 days post-operatively: moderate-to-severe pain (often worse than immediate postoperative pain), foul odor from the socket, and visible empty socket without organizing blood clot when viewed in a mirror.

**Prevention strategies** are more effective than treatment: avoid smoking for at least 72 hours post-operatively (preferably longer); avoid vigorous rinsing or spitting for 24 hours; avoid drinking through straws; and follow your surgeon's postoperative instructions regarding socket protection. Chlorhexidine rinses prescribed by your surgeon reduce risk by 25-40%.

**Management** if dry socket develops: contact your surgeon, who will gently rinse the socket and place medicinal packing that provides pain relief typically within 30 minutes. Packing is changed every 24-48 hours until healing progresses. NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600-800 mg every 6-8 hours) provide the best pain relief for dry socket.

## Neurosensory Complications (Altered Sensation)

After wisdom tooth extraction or other oral surgery, temporary numbness of the lower lip, chin, tongue, or other areas is common. This typically resolves within 2-8 weeks as nerve function recovers. **Prevention of self-injury** during the period of numbness is important: be careful when eating (easy to bite lip/cheek without feeling it), avoid very hot foods/beverages (burns go unnoticed), and be cautious with oral hygiene (avoid injuring gingiva with toothbrush).

**Permanent** altered sensation is rare (0.5-2% of patients undergoing wisdom tooth extraction) but should be discussed during your preoperative informed consent. Notify your surgeon immediately if sensation is not showing gradual improvement after 8 weeks, or if numbness is expanding to new areas.

## Healing Timeline—What to Expect

**Days 1-3**: Maximum pain and swelling; pale/white appearance of extraction socket (normal blood clot); frequent oozing when rinsing; difficulty opening mouth fully.

**Days 4-7**: Pain gradually decreasing; swelling starting to resolve; extraction socket begins organizing with yellowish granulation tissue; mouth opening improving; normal activities gradually resumable.

**Days 8-14**: Pain minimal except with direct pressure; swelling substantially resolved; socket continuing to organize with pink granulation tissue; minor oozing when rinsing only; return to normal diet acceptable (avoiding very hard/hot foods).

**Weeks 3-6**: Complete healing of soft tissues; socket filled with granulation tissue; epithelialization complete; bone remodeling beginning.

**Weeks 7-12**: Bone remodeling progressing; socket dimension gradually decreasing as bone fills defect; stability improving; if planning implant, osseous baseline typically achieved by week 12.

## When to Return for Follow-up

Contact your surgeon for follow-up examination if:
- Pain worsens or changes character after initial improvement
- Swelling increases after day 3
- Fever develops
- Drainage or foul odor occurs
- Numbness/tingling is not improving after 4-6 weeks
- Any signs concerning you develop

Most surgeons schedule a 1-week postoperative appointment to assess healing. Attend this appointment even if feeling well; early identification of developing complications enables prompt treatment and improved outcomes.

## Conclusion

Most patients experience uncomplicated healing after oral surgery with gradual pain reduction, swelling resolution, and return to normal function over 7-14 days. Understanding normal postoperative symptoms versus warning signs of complications enables you to recognize problems early and seek appropriate care. Following postoperative instructions regarding activity restrictions, medication use, oral hygiene, and dietary modification optimizes healing and minimizes complication risk. Maintain close communication with your surgical team if any concerning symptoms develop, as early intervention typically results in more favorable outcomes than delayed care.
