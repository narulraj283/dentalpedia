Accurate diagnosis of periodontal disease requires specific clinical measurements and radiographic assessment. Understanding these diagnostic tests helps you appreciate the detailed evaluation involved in determining gum disease presence and severity, and guides appropriate treatment planning.

## The Periodontal Probing Examination

The periodontal probing examination is the primary diagnostic tool for assessing periodontal disease. Your dentist uses a specialized periodontal probe—a thin, graduated instrument marked at millimeter intervals—to systematically measure the depth of the space between the tooth and gum (periodontal pocket).

The probe is gently inserted into the crevice between tooth and gum, and the depth is recorded at six locations around each tooth (mesiobuccal, buccal, distobuccal on the outer surface and mesiolingual, lingual, distolingual on the inner surface).

In healthy mouths, probing depths measure 1 to 3 millimeters. Depths exceeding 3 millimeters indicate potential gum disease. Depths of 4 to 6 millimeters indicate moderate periodontitis, while depths exceeding 6 millimeters indicate advanced disease.

## Bleeding on Probing

An important part of the probing examination is noting whether bleeding occurs during probing. Healthy gums don't bleed when gently probed. Bleeding indicates inflammation and is often the earliest clinical sign of gum disease.

The absence of bleeding is an excellent prognostic sign—it often indicates gum disease is absent or controlled by treatment. Persistent bleeding despite treatment indicates inadequate disease control.

## Clinical Attachment Loss

Clinical attachment loss (CAL) measures the distance from a fixed anatomical reference point (the cementoenamel junction where the crown meets the root) to the deepest part of the pocket. This measurement indicates true periodontal damage, as opposed to simple pocket depth which can be influenced by gum swelling.

CAL greater than 1 to 2 millimeters indicates periodontal disease. Progressive CAL over time indicates worsening disease. Stable or improving CAL indicates successful treatment.

## Gingival Recession Assessment

Gingival recession (loss of gum tissue) is measured as the distance from the gumline to the cementoenamel junction. Combined with pocket depth, recession provides a complete picture of periodontal status.

Some teeth have pockets with recession (indicating attachment loss from periodontal disease). Others have gum that has receded but shallow pockets remain, indicating the recession represents gum tissue loss rather than progressive disease.

## Radiographic Evaluation

Radiographs (X-rays) reveal bone levels supporting the teeth. Healthy bone typically reaches to within 2 millimeters of the cementoenamel junction. Bone loss is evident when this distance increases, showing resorbed bone margins below normal levels.

Radiographs reveal the pattern of bone loss. Horizontal bone loss (affecting all surfaces equally) differs from vertical bone loss (affecting some surfaces more than others). Vertical bone loss patterns often indicate more aggressive disease.

Radiographs are essential for assessing whether apparent deep pockets actually have bone loss (indicating true periodontitis) or whether pockets reflect simple gum swelling without bone loss (indicating gingivitis only).

## Computerized Measurements

Digital imaging allows enhanced analysis of radiographic bone levels. Software programs can quantify bone loss using reference landmarks, allowing precise tracking of changes over time.

3D cone-beam computed tomography (CBCT) provides three-dimensional visualization of bone anatomy, particularly useful for surgical planning or assessment of complex cases.

## Plaque and Calculus Assessment

Your dentist assesses the amount and location of plaque (bacterial biofilm) and calculus (tartar). High plaque scores indicate inadequate home care. Calculus distribution guides treatment planning—areas with heavy calculus require more aggressive scaling.

## Mobility Testing

Tooth mobility (looseness) is assessed by attempting to move teeth in different directions with an instrument or fingers. Excessive mobility indicates significant bone loss compromising tooth support.

Mobility assessments help determine prognosis—severely mobile teeth may have poor long-term retention prospects despite treatment.

## Microbial Testing

Advanced practice may include bacterial identification through culture or molecular testing. Identifying specific pathogenic bacteria can guide antibiotic selection or confirm aggressive periodontitis.

However, microbial testing is not routine—clinical signs and measurements provide sufficient information for most cases.

## Genetic Testing

Some practitioners offer genetic testing to identify IL-1 polymorphisms associated with aggressive periodontitis. However, clinical utility remains debated and insurance rarely covers this testing.

## Blood Tests and Biomarkers

Research explores whether blood tests measuring inflammatory markers or periodontal-specific proteins could identify periodontal disease or predict progression. However, these tests are not yet standard clinical practice.

Saliva testing for bacterial levels or inflammatory markers is an area of ongoing research.

## Full Mouth Examination Documentation

A comprehensive periodontal evaluation documents findings for each tooth. This includes pocket depths at six locations, bleeding on probing, attachment loss, recession, and notes about mobility or other findings.

Documentation allows tracking changes over time. Comparing baseline measurements to follow-up measurements determines whether treatment is effective or disease is progressing despite intervention.

## Risk Assessment Tools

Some practices use risk assessment tools that combine clinical findings, radiographic evidence, and patient factors (smoking, diabetes, family history) to quantify periodontal disease risk and guide treatment intensity.

These tools help determine whether standard maintenance intervals are appropriate or more aggressive monitoring is needed.

## Patient Communication of Findings

Your dentist should explain findings clearly using models, radiographs, or computer images. Understanding pocket depths, bone loss patterns, and prognosis helps motivate necessary treatment compliance.

Some dentists use periodontal photographs documenting visible disease, providing visual motivation for treatment.

## Repeat Measurement After Treatment

After active treatment like scaling and root planing, repeat probing assessment determines whether treatment was successful. Reduced pocket depths and absence of bleeding indicate successful treatment response.

Stable measurements over multiple maintenance visits indicate adequate disease control.

## Limitations of Diagnostic Tools

Despite sophisticated diagnostic tests, clinical decision-making remains somewhat subjective. Two dentists might assess the same patient differently. This is why second opinions are sometimes sought for complex cases.

Measurements can be influenced by patient factors—inflammation, swelling, and moisture content affect measurements. This is why baseline measurements establish reference points for tracking changes rather than being interpreted in isolation.

Understanding the battery of diagnostic tests and measurements used to assess periodontal disease provides insight into how dentists determine disease presence and severity. These objective measurements guide treatment planning and track responses to therapy. While sophisticated, these tools remain most valuable when combined with clinical judgment and patient compliance with preventive care.
