## Why Wisdom Teeth Are Removed

Wisdom teeth, also called third molars, are the last permanent teeth to erupt, typically appearing between ages 17 and 25. Many people require extraction of one or more wisdom teeth for various reasons. The most common reason is insufficient space in the jaw, which causes wisdom teeth to become impacted—stuck beneath the gum line or bone and unable to erupt fully. Impacted wisdom teeth can damage adjacent teeth, create food traps that promote decay, cause cysts, or develop infections.

Partially erupted wisdom teeth pose special challenges because their position makes them difficult to clean with a toothbrush or floss, leading to rapid decay and gum disease. Crowding caused by erupting wisdom teeth can compromise orthodontic results or require further orthodontic treatment. Some dentists recommend preventive extraction of wisdom teeth that are functional and healthy to avoid problems later, though this remains somewhat controversial.

## Pre-Extraction Evaluation

Before wisdom teeth extraction, your dentist or oral surgeon will perform a thorough evaluation. This includes a clinical examination to assess the position and angulation of your wisdom teeth, assessment of adjacent structures, and evaluation of bone and soft tissue relationships. Panoramic radiographs, and sometimes three-dimensional cone beam computed tomography (CBCT), are used to visualize tooth position, root structure, and proximity to the inferior alveolar nerve and maxillary sinus.

During the pre-operative consultation, discuss your medical history, current medications, and any allergies or anesthesia concerns. Your surgeon will explain the procedure, potential risks and complications, and what to expect during recovery. Pre-operative blood work or testing may be recommended based on your age, medical status, and the complexity of the planned extraction.

## The Extraction Procedure

Wisdom teeth extraction is typically performed under local anesthesia, nitrous oxide and local anesthesia, or general anesthesia, depending on the complexity of the case and patient preference. Your oral surgeon will discuss anesthesia options during the consultation. The procedure usually takes 20 to 90 minutes depending on the number of teeth being extracted and their position.

For simple extractions of erupted teeth, your surgeon elevates soft tissue, uses an elevator to loosen the tooth, and then extracts it with forceps. For impacted wisdom teeth, bone removal and tooth sectioning may be necessary. Your surgeon will carefully preserve the surrounding bone and soft tissues, working methodically to minimize trauma. If stitches are needed, either absorbable or removable sutures will be placed.

## Immediate Post-Operative Period

You will spend recovery time in the dental office under observation, typically 30 to 60 minutes. Gauze packs are placed in the extraction sockets to control bleeding, and you'll bite gently on them for 30 to 45 minutes after surgery. It's normal to experience some bleeding for the first 24 hours. The surgical sites may ooze slightly when you change gauze or during sleep.

Pain and swelling increase gradually during the first three days after extraction and then gradually improve. You may experience trismus (limited mouth opening) for several days, making eating and speaking slightly uncomfortable. Bruising and swelling are expected and peak around day two or three. Ice application during the first 24 hours reduces swelling; after 24 hours, heat application becomes more effective.

## Managing Pain and Swelling

Prescription or over-the-counter pain medications will help manage discomfort. Take medications as prescribed and don't exceed the recommended dosage. Many patients find that alternating between different pain relievers provides better relief. Ice packs applied for 20 minutes with 20 minutes off during the first 24 hours significantly reduce swelling and pain.

Elevation of your head above heart level, even while sleeping, helps reduce swelling. Sleep with an extra pillow or two propped behind your head. Avoid strenuous activity, vigorous rinsing, and drinking through straws during the first week, as these can dislodge blood clots and cause dry socket, a painful complication. Smoking is strongly discouraged, as it significantly increases complications.

## Dietary Considerations

Eat soft, cool foods during the first few days, such as yogurt, applesauce, ice cream, smoothies, pudding, and mashed potatoes. Avoid hot foods and beverages for the first week. Stay well-hydrated but avoid drinking through straws. After the first few days, gradually return to normal foods as your comfort level improves. Avoid hard, crunchy, sticky, and spicy foods for at least one week, as these can irritate the surgical sites.

Chew on the opposite side of your mouth from the surgical sites. Don't force food into extraction areas. Adequate nutrition and hydration promote healing, so try to consume balanced meals despite temporary dietary limitations.

## Oral Hygiene During Recovery

Gentle oral hygiene is important for promoting healing and preventing infection. Avoid disturbing the extraction sites for the first 24 hours. After the first day, you may gently rinse with warm salt water (one-half teaspoon salt in eight ounces water) after meals and before bed. This gentle rinsing removes food debris and promotes healing without disturbing blood clots.

Brush your other teeth normally, but avoid the extraction sites. Don't use mouthwash or hydrogen peroxide rinses during the first week, as these can disturb healing tissues. After one week, resume normal oral hygiene including flossing, being gentle around the extraction sites.

## Healing Timeline and Complications

Complete healing of extraction sockets takes several months, though you'll feel substantially better within one to two weeks. Bone healing continues for months after extraction. Most patients return to normal activities within a few days, though strenuous exercise should be avoided for at least five to seven days.

Possible complications include dry socket (delayed bone healing causing severe pain), infection, prolonged numbness, and sinus opening if upper wisdom teeth are extracted. Contact your surgeon immediately if you experience fever, excessive swelling, difficulty breathing or swallowing, or pain that worsens after several days. Post-operative numbness usually resolves within weeks but can occasionally persist longer.

## Replacement Considerations

Once healing is complete, missing wisdom teeth typically don't require replacement since they're not essential for chewing or speaking. However, if other molars are missing, replacement through dental implants or a bridge may be considered to maintain proper bite and prevent adjacent tooth movement.
