## Your First Week After Root Canal Treatment

After your root canal procedure, your tooth needs time to heal. Think of your mouth like you would any other part of your body that's just gone through a medical procedure—it needs protection and gentle care while it recovers. Most people can return to their normal activities right after treatment, but your tooth itself needs special attention for about a week.

Your dentist gave you specific instructions for your particular situation, and those are your primary guide. But here's what generally happens during the recovery process so you know what to expect and how to help your tooth heal best.

## Managing Pain in the First Few Days

Ibuprofen is your best friend right now. Most dentists recommend starting with 600 mg of ibuprofen, which you can take every 6 hours if needed. Acetaminophen at 650 mg works too, and you can alternate between them—ibuprofen at 9am and 3pm, then acetaminophen at 12pm and 6pm, for example. This combination gives you better pain control than either alone. Don't wait until pain develops; start taking pain medication before the numbing medication wears off.

For the first 24-48 hours, your pain usually gets worse before it gets better. This is normal. Your tooth's surrounding tissues are responding to the treatment by creating inflammation as part of the healing process. By day three, most people notice improvement. By day seven, most people feel significantly better.

Take your pain medication with food to protect your stomach. Ice packs on your cheek for 15 minutes at a time, several times during the first 24 hours, can reduce pain and swelling. After the first day, warmth might feel better than cold. A warm compress feels soothing to many people.

## What You Can and Can't Eat

During the first week, stick to soft foods that don't require much chewing. Your treated tooth is sensitive and your jaw might be sore from holding your mouth open during the procedure. Soft pasta, soup, yogurt, mashed potatoes, scrambled eggs, and ice cream are all fine choices.

Avoid hot foods and hot drinks for at least 24 hours. Your tooth is extra sensitive to temperature right now, and heat can trigger pain. Cold foods and drinks feel better anyway. Also avoid hard, crunchy, or sticky foods—these require chewing right where your tooth is tender, and sticky foods might dislodge any temporary restoration your dentist placed.

Don't chew on the treated side of your mouth. Even though the instinct is to test whether the tooth still hurts, resisting that urge helps healing go faster. Chewing on that tooth puts pressure on it during the critical healing phase. Stick to soft foods and chew on the other side for about a week.

Avoid alcohol for at least 48 hours after treatment, especially if you were given prescription pain medication. Alcohol interferes with healing and can interact with medications. Even without medication, alcohol impairs healing processes, so staying away from it helps your tooth recover faster.

## Oral Hygiene During Healing

You can brush your other teeth normally, but be very gentle around the treated tooth. Use a soft-bristled toothbrush—not a medium or hard one. Rinse gently after brushing rather than vigorously rinsing. Avoid flossing right around the treated tooth for at least a few days if your dentist placed a temporary restoration. You don't want to disturb it.

If your dentist gave you a chlorhexidine rinse or other special rinse, use it as directed. Most people rinse very gently twice daily rather than using vigorous rinsing motions. Your mouth is healing, so gentle care is better than aggressive cleaning right now.

## Temporary Restoration Care

If your dentist placed a temporary restoration (temporary filling or crown), be careful not to dislodge it. Don't chew on that tooth, and avoid sticky foods. A temporary restoration is designed to protect your tooth while you're healing and between appointments. If it comes out before your next appointment, contact your dentist. The tooth's opening is now exposed and could become infected or the tooth could be damaged.

Watch for the temporary restoration coming loose during the first few days. Sometimes it loosens slightly and you notice it with your tongue. If it feels loose but still in place, you might be able to manage until your next appointment, but call your dentist to be sure. If it comes completely out, try not to let food or bacteria enter the opening. Contact your dentist immediately to get it replaced.

## Managing Swelling and Other Symptoms

Some swelling in your face, gums, or jaw is normal during the first few days. Your body is healing. Ice in the first 24 hours helps control swelling. Swelling usually peaks around 24-48 hours after treatment, then gradually decreases. By day 5-7, most swelling is gone.

If swelling seems to be getting worse rather than better after the first 48 hours, contact your dentist. Similarly, if swelling is severe, makes it hard to swallow or breathe, or extends beyond your jaw area, call your dentist. These could indicate an infection needing treatment.

Some people experience slight discoloration or bruising inside their mouth. This usually comes from the rubber dam or other equipment used during treatment. It's not concerning and fades within a few days.

## When to Contact Your Dentist

Call your dentist if you develop a fever above 101°F. A slight elevation in temperature is less concerning, but 101°F or higher suggests your body is fighting an infection. Call if you develop severe swelling that's spreading beyond where you expect it, or if swelling makes it difficult to swallow or breathe.

Contact them if pain doesn't improve after 48-72 hours, or if it suddenly worsens after it started improving. Severe pain that doesn't respond to ibuprofen and acetaminophen taken together needs professional evaluation. If the temporary restoration falls out, contact them immediately.

Also call if you develop a sore or ulcer in your mouth that isn't healing, or if you have any drainage (pus). These signs suggest infection needing treatment. Bad taste in your mouth isn't concerning and usually indicates your mouth is cleaning out healing tissue.

## Your Follow-up Appointments

Your dentist scheduled you for follow-up appointments for good reasons. Initial appointments check your healing and confirm everything is going well. Don't skip these—they verify your recovery is progressing normally and address any problems early.

Your dentist will likely place a permanent restoration (crown or filling) within 2-4 weeks. Don't delay this. Your treated tooth is like a tooth with a large cavity—without proper restoration, bacteria can enter and re-infect your tooth. Also, uncrowned teeth are more likely to break because they're weaker after root canal treatment.

Radiographs taken at follow-up appointments show whether your tooth is healing properly. Healing happens slowly—it can take 3-6 months for complete healing of the bone around your tooth. Your dentist monitors this progress to ensure everything heals correctly.

## Long-Term Care of Your Treated Tooth

After you return to normal activities, remember that your treated tooth is now more fragile than a healthy tooth. Avoid using it as a tool to open things or bite down on hard objects. Don't chew ice or hard candy. Wear a mouthguard if you play contact sports—a treated tooth that gets hit can fracture more easily than a healthy tooth.

Maintain excellent oral hygiene. Brush twice daily with a soft toothbrush, floss daily, and visit your dentist regularly. A treated tooth can still develop cavities, and cavities near treated teeth are particularly problematic because they can re-infect the tooth.

## Conclusion

Your recovery from root canal treatment is usually straightforward. Most people experience mild to moderate pain for about a week, which responds well to over-the-counter pain medication combined with gentle care. By following your dentist's instructions, avoiding chewing on the treated tooth, managing pain proactively, and attending follow-up appointments, you give your tooth the best chance to heal properly. Within a few weeks, you'll be back to normal, with a saved tooth that can last the rest of your life with proper care.

## Key Takeaway

Recovery from root canal treatment takes about a week of gentle care—manage pain with ibuprofen and acetaminophen, eat soft foods, avoid chewing on the treated tooth, and attend all follow-up appointments for proper restoration.
