## What Is Peri-Implantitis?

Peri-implantitis is bacterial infection and inflammation of tissues and bone surrounding dental implants. The name parallels periodontitis (gum disease affecting natural teeth), but peri-implantitis is a distinct disease with different characteristics and more rapid progression.

Peri-implantitis affects 10-40% of patients with implants at some point in their implant lifetime. While many cases respond well to treatment, some progress to implant loss if untreated.

Understanding peri-implantitis causes, early signs, and treatment options allows early intervention and prevention of implant loss.

## Why Implants Are Vulnerable to Peri-Implantitis

Natural teeth are protected by the periodontal ligament—a specialized connective tissue that attaches teeth to bone and provides an immune barrier. Implants lack this protective structure. Bone directly interfaces with the implant surface.

If bacteria breach the gum attachment around an implant, direct access to bone occurs. Unlike natural teeth where the periodontal ligament resists bacterial spread, implants offer little resistance to bacterial progression into bone.

This anatomical difference means bacterial infection progresses faster around implants than around natural teeth. Untreated peri-implantitis can destroy bone support and lead to implant loss within months—much faster than periodontitis leading to tooth loss.

## Causes and Risk Factors

Peri-implantitis develops when bacteria accumulate on implant surfaces. Inadequate oral hygiene is the primary cause. Daily flossing and brushing prevent plaque accumulation; neglect allows biofilm development.

Risk factors include:
- Poor oral hygiene (most significant)
- History of periodontitis (bacterial species from former gum disease persist in mouth)
- Smoking (impairs immune function)
- Diabetes (affects immune response and bone healing)
- Immunosuppression
- Excessive forces on implants (bruxism, heavy chewing)

Implant design factors may contribute—surface roughness affects bacterial colonization. Some implant surfaces accumulate plaque more readily than others.

Abutment and crown margins at or below the gingival (gum) line accumulate bacteria more readily than margins above gum line. Subgingival margins require meticulous cleaning.

## Clinical Signs and Symptoms

Early peri-implantitis signs include:
- Bleeding when flossing around implant
- Swelling or redness around implant
- Discomfort or tenderness around implant

Advanced signs include:
- Suppuration (pus) around implant
- Implant mobility (looseness)
- Progressive swelling
- Sinus tract (drainage channel)

Some patients experience no symptoms—disease progresses silently until advanced. This emphasizes importance of professional monitoring.

Radiographic evidence of bone loss around implant indicates peri-implantitis. Bone loss is distinguished from normal resorption (0.4-0.8mm first year, then 0.05-0.1mm annually) by its progressive pattern.

## Diagnosis and Assessment

Clinical examination reveals peri-implantitis signs. Practitioners assess:
- Bleeding when probing around implant (healthy implants shouldn't bleed)
- Pocket depth around implant (should be 1-3mm; deeper pockets suggest disease)
- Implant mobility (healthy implants are completely stable)
- Suppuration

Radiographs document bone loss pattern. Progressive bone loss between radiographs taken 6-12 months apart indicates active disease.

## Prevention: The Best Treatment

Prevention is infinitely superior to treating established peri-implantitis. Prevention strategies:

1. **Daily oral hygiene**: Brush twice daily with soft-bristled toothbrush; floss or water floss daily; use interdental brushes.

2. **Professional cleaning**: Professional cleanings every 6-12 months remove calculus deposits.

3. **Smoking cessation**: Smoking significantly increases peri-implantitis risk. Quitting protects implants.

4. **Systemic disease control**: Controlling diabetes and managing autoimmune conditions reduces infection risk.

5. **Avoid trauma**: Protect implants from excessive forces through mouthguards if grinding present.

6. **Regular monitoring**: Annual or biannual professional assessment detects early disease before advanced loss occurs.

Patients who follow these preventive strategies rarely develop peri-implantitis.

## Early-Stage Treatment

Early peri-implantitis (detected before extensive bone loss) responds well to conservative treatment.

Professional cleaning focuses on removing biofilm and calculus. Specialized instruments designed for implants (plastic or titanium curettes) remove deposits without damaging implant surfaces or abutments.

Antimicrobial therapy supplements mechanical cleaning. Chlorhexidine rinses (short-term) or local antimicrobial delivery systems applied at treatment time reduce bacterial burden.

Patient education emphasizes improved home care and oral hygiene compliance. Many early cases resolve with professional cleaning and improved home care alone.

## Advanced-Stage Treatment

Advanced peri-implantitis (extensive bone loss, pocket depths >5mm, possible implant mobility) may require surgical intervention.

Surgical debridement involves accessing bone around implant, removing infected tissue and granulation tissue, and decontaminating implant surface. The implant is carefully cleaned using specialized techniques that avoid damaging its surface.

Bone regeneration materials (grafts, membranes, growth factors) may be placed to promote bone healing. These procedures are complex and require experienced surgeons.

Following surgical treatment, healing periods (3-6 months) are necessary before re-evaluation. Success depends on post-operative care and improved home care preventing re-infection.

## When Implants May Be Lost

Some cases with advanced peri-implantitis don't respond to treatment. Progressive bone loss continues despite treatment attempts. When bone loss becomes too extensive, implant stability cannot be maintained.

Implant mobility despite treatment indicates failure. Mobile implants cannot be retained safely—removal is necessary.

Some practitioners attempt salvage of severely compromised implants; others recommend removal and replacement after healing.

## Post-Treatment Maintenance

After peri-implantitis treatment, continued vigilance is critical. These patients are at higher risk for recurrent disease.

More frequent professional appointments (every 3-6 months versus annual) monitor for early recurrence. Stricter home care regimens are necessary.

Some practitioners recommend adjunctive antimicrobial therapy—periodic antimicrobial rinses or even local antimicrobial agents placed periodically around implants.

## Implant Loss and Replacement

If peri-implantitis leads to implant loss, removal is necessary. After removal, the extraction site heals (3-6 months). Bone resorption commonly occurs at extraction sites.

Replacement implant placement may require bone grafting if insufficient bone remains. New implant placement is sometimes delayed until complete healing and bone assessment.

Prevention of recurrent peri-implantitis in the replacement implant is essential—improved home care and more frequent professional monitoring reduce recurrence risk.

## Long-Term Prognosis

Most peri-implantitis cases can be managed successfully with early detection and appropriate treatment. Late-detected or neglected cases have less favorable prognosis.

Long-term studies show that with successful treatment and excellent home care, implants can continue functioning long-term. However, these patients require lifetime vigilant maintenance.

## Your Role in Prevention

Your commitment to oral hygiene and professional monitoring is the most important factor preventing peri-implantitis. Daily flossing, careful brushing, professional cleanings, and regular check-ups virtually eliminate peri-implantitis risk.

Report any concerning symptoms immediately to your dentist. Early intervention prevents implant loss and major treatment.
