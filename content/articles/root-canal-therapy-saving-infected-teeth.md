## What Happens When Your Tooth Gets Infected?

Your tooth has a living nerve inside it. When bacteria from decay reach that nerve, they cause infection. Your tooth responds with pain, swelling, and inflammation. If left untreated, the infection spreads beyond your tooth—into your jawbone, potentially into the sinuses or facial tissues, even into your bloodstream in serious cases.

A root canal saves your tooth by removing the infected nerve tissue, thoroughly cleaning out the infection, and then sealing the space so bacteria can't re-enter. It's not complicated surgery—it's careful cleaning and sealing of the interior of your tooth. The name comes from the term "root canal"—the space inside your tooth from the crown down through the roots where the nerve lives.

## Why Your Tooth Is Worth Saving

Losing a tooth creates long-term problems. An empty space allows your other teeth to shift out of position, changing your bite and creating cosmetic issues. Your jaw structure can gradually resorb (disappear) without a tooth's root stimulating the bone. Replacing a tooth with an implant or bridge is more expensive and takes longer than treating it with a root canal. Your natural tooth, even after a root canal, is almost always the best option if treatment can succeed.

Modern root canal therapy succeeds in 85-95% of cases. That's an excellent success rate that justifies trying to save your tooth. Extraction is permanent and irreversible. A root canal is actually a way of giving your tooth another chance at functioning for many years.

## The Step-by-Step Process

Your dentist starts by examining your tooth and taking X-rays to see the roots and understand the infection's extent. They apply numbing medication so you're completely comfortable. Unlike the scary reputation root canals have, the procedure itself is painless—you won't feel pain during treatment because the tooth is numb. In fact, many people feel better during the procedure because they're finally addressing the source of their pain.

Your dentist places a rubber dam—a small rubber sheet with a hole for your tooth—around your tooth. This keeps your mouth dry, protects you from accidentally swallowing instruments or medicament, and gives your dentist a clear working area. It might feel strange, but it's a safety feature you'll appreciate.

Your dentist creates an opening in the crown of your tooth to access the pulp chamber (the main space containing the nerve tissue). They then use special instruments to remove the infected nerve tissue. As they work deeper into the tooth, they determine working length—how far down the canals go—using electronic devices and X-rays.

## Cleaning and Shaping Your Canals

Your dentist uses progressively larger instruments to shape and clean the root canals. Modern instruments are made of nickel-titanium, a flexible material that works well in curved canals without breaking. They carefully enlarge and shape the canals while staying within the root boundaries—not going too far and damaging the apex (root tip) or going too short and leaving infected material behind.

During this process, your dentist irrigates (flushes) the canals with antimicrobial solutions that kill bacteria and help dissolve infected tissue. Different irrigation solutions work in different ways—sodium hypochlorite dissolves organic tissue, EDTA dissolves mineral deposits, and chlorhexidine has antimicrobial properties. Using multiple irrigation solutions sequentially provides better disinfection than any single agent alone.

## Filling Your Tooth Completely

Once the canals are shaped and thoroughly cleaned, your dentist fills them with gutta-percha—a rubber-like material that won't degrade over time and won't support bacterial growth. Gutta-percha is combined with sealer, a cement that fills any tiny spaces between gutta-percha and your canal walls, creating a complete seal.

Your dentist confirms the filling extends all the way to the apex (the end of the root) by taking an X-ray. Complete filling is crucial—any empty space could allow bacteria to survive and cause future problems. They remove any excess filling material from above the tooth surface, then seal the access opening with a temporary restoration.

## Temporary versus Permanent Restoration

You'll have a temporary filling or crown protecting your tooth between appointments. This temporary restoration is just that—temporary. It protects your tooth from bacteria entering while you're waiting for your permanent restoration, but it's not strong enough to last long-term.

Within 2-4 weeks after root canal treatment, your tooth needs a permanent restoration. This is crucial because your tooth is now hollow inside (the space where the nerve was) and more fragile than before. The permanent crown protects it from breaking and prevents bacteria from seeping back inside. Many root canal failures are actually caused by leaky restorations, not by the root canal treatment itself.

## Why Modern Techniques Are So Much Better

Old root canal procedures were slower, more uncomfortable, and less successful. Modern techniques use:

- Electronic apical locators that determine working length precisely, reducing the number of X-rays needed
- Flexible nickel-titanium instruments that work well even in curved canals
- Advanced irrigation systems that thoroughly clean canals
- Rapid instrumentation techniques that reduce appointment time

These advances mean modern root canals are faster, more comfortable, and more successful than those done even 10 years ago.

## What to Expect Afterward

Mild to moderate discomfort for a few days is normal—your body is healing from the procedure. Pain medication (ibuprofen and acetaminophen work great) controls this discomfort. Avoid chewing on the tooth while it heals. Most people feel significantly better within a week.

Your tooth will need a permanent crown or filling to protect it. Your permanent restoration should be completed within 4 weeks to provide optimal protection. Following your dentist's instructions regarding food, oral hygiene, and pain management during healing ensures the best outcome.

## Long-Term Success and Care

After root canal treatment, your tooth is no longer "alive"—it doesn't have a nerve. But it's still your tooth, still functional, and can remain so for decades with proper care. Brush it like your other teeth, floss around it, and see your dentist regularly. Avoid using it to open things or chew on hard objects (which could fracture it).

Your root canal-treated tooth should function just like your other teeth. You can eat normally, bite with normal force, and the tooth shouldn't cause problems. Most people don't even notice which tooth had the root canal after a few months—it just becomes a normal part of their mouth again.

## Preventing Future Problems

Once your tooth has had a root canal, the best strategy is preventing the need for retreatment. Maintain excellent oral hygiene to prevent new cavities. Avoid chewing on hard objects that could crack your restoration or tooth. See your dentist regularly for checkups. If your crown develops damage or decay, have it replaced promptly.

Some people get occasional slight twinges of tenderness from root canal-treated teeth, especially if they bite down hard. This usually settles quickly. But persistent pain or swelling warrants contact with your dentist to ensure nothing's going wrong.

## Conclusion

Root canal therapy is a highly successful way to save your tooth from extraction. Modern techniques make the procedure quick and relatively comfortable. The key to long-term success is getting a good permanent restoration promptly and maintaining excellent oral care afterward. Most root canal-treated teeth function perfectly for many years or decades, making it an excellent choice for preserving your natural tooth.

## Key Takeaway

Root canal treatment removes infected nerve tissue and seals your tooth, saving it from extraction with an 85-95% success rate. Getting a permanent crown afterward and maintaining good oral hygiene keeps your tooth healthy long-term.
