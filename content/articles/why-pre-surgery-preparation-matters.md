What you do before surgery is as important as the surgery itself. Proper prep reduces complications by 70%. Your dentist checks your health, reviews medications, takes special X-rays, and gives you fasting and antibiotic instructions. These steps matter for a safe surgery and smooth recovery.

## Medical Risk Assessment and ASA Classification

Your dentist assesses your health using a system that classifies patients from healthy to very sick. Your health status determines if surgery is safe and what special precautions are needed. For example, if you have high blood pressure, your dentist checks it before surgery. Diabetics need blood sugar checked.

## Comprehensive Medical History and Medication Management

Your dentist reviews all your medications. Some medications affect bleeding or healing. Tell your dentist about blood thinners, heart medications, or bone-building drugs. Some medications need to continue before surgery; others don't.

## Imaging and Diagnostic Planning

Your dentist takes special X-rays to see exactly where nerves, sinuses, and bones are. For simple extractions, regular X-rays work. For implants, special 3D scans show bone quality and structure needed for success. These images guide the dentist to perform surgery safely.

## Antibiotic Prophylaxis Protocols

Your dentist may give you antibiotics before surgery to prevent infection. A single dose 1 hour before surgery reduces infection risk from 8-12% to 1-2%. If you're allergic to penicillin, a different antibiotic is used.

## Pre-operative Fasting and Sedation Considerations

If you're getting sedation or sleep, you must fast beforehand. Follow your dentist's instructions: typically 6 hours for solid food, 2 hours for clear liquids. If you have reflux or are obese, tell your dentist—you may need different fasting rules.

## Oral Hygiene and Preoperative Chlorhexidine Rinses

For major surgery, your dentist may give you antimicrobial rinses to use before surgery. These reduce bacteria by 99.9% and lower infection risk. For simple extractions, regular rinses at home are fine.

## Timing and Scheduling Considerations

Morning surgery is better than afternoon. Your blood clots better in the morning, which helps with bleeding control and healing.

Elective surgical scheduling should avoid periods of acute infection or immunosuppression. Patients with active upper respiratory infections demonstrate elevated serum inflammatory markers and temporary impaired neutrophil chemotaxis, increasing infection risk 2-3 fold. Rescheduling should be considered until respiratory symptoms resolve completely, typically 7-10 days post-symptom onset.

## Pre-operative Laboratory Testing and Clearance

Laboratory testing requirements vary by patient ASA classification and surgical complexity. ASA I and II patients undergoing uncomplicated extractions typically require no laboratory testing. ASA III patients or those with concerning medical history may benefit from baseline CBC (complete blood count) and metabolic panel assessing renal function and electrolyte balance.

Patients on warfarin anticoagulation require INR (International Normalized Ratio) testing within 24 hours of surgery, with therapeutic range 2.0-3.0 typically acceptable for oral surgical procedures. PT (prothrombin time) and PTT (partial thromboplastin time) assessment may be indicated in patients with bleeding disorder history or unexplained bleeding tendency. Medical consultation and clearance from patient's primary care physician or appropriate specialist ensures coordinated care and surgical safety.

## Preparing for Your Specific Situation

Your dentist will give you individualized pre-operative instructions based on your health, medications, and the type of surgery. Follow these instructions carefully. If you have questions about why you need to do something—fast before surgery, take special medications, or get lab tests—ask your dentist. Understanding the reasons helps you comply more carefully.

*Every patient's situation is unique. Talk to your dentist about the best approach for your specific needs.*

Good preparation reduces your risk of problems during and after surgery. It also helps you heal faster. Most of the steps are simple—stop certain medicines, avoid food before your appointment, and arrange for a ride home.

Talk to your dentist about any health conditions or medicines you take. Some blood thinners, supplements, and herbal products can increase bleeding during surgery. Your dentist needs a complete picture of your health to keep you safe.

## Conclusion

Pre-operative preparation isn't just a bureaucratic hoop to jump through—it's the foundation for safe surgery and smooth recovery. Your dentist's careful assessment of your health, review of your medications, and imaging analysis enable them to anticipate problems and plan a surgical approach that's safest for your specific situation. Following pre-operative instructions carefully, from fasting to medication management to scheduling lab work, shows how much you're investing in your own successful outcome.

> **Key Takeaway:** Comprehensive pre-operative preparation reduces surgical complications by up to 70% and improves healing outcomes. Understand your specific health risks, follow all pre-operative instructions carefully, disclose all medications and health conditions honestly, and ask questions about anything you don't understand. The time and effort you invest in preparation directly translates to safer surgery and faster recovery.
