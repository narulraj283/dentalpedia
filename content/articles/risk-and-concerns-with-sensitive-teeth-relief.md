## Why Your Teeth Hurt and What You Can Do

Sensitive teeth are frustrating. That sharp pain when you drink cold water or bite into ice cream can make you avoid things you enjoy. Understanding why your teeth are sensitive helps you find the right solution. Many cases of tooth sensitivity are preventable or treatable.

## Understanding Tooth Sensitivity

Professional treatments, including [fluoride applications](/articles/risk-and-concerns-with-fluoride-benefits/), can also help. Your tooth has three layers. The hard outer layer is enamel. Underneath is dentin. The innermost part contains the nerve. Tooth sensitivity happens when dentin is exposed because enamel has worn away or your gum has receded. When exposed dentin contacts cold, hot, sweet, or acidic substances, or when you brush over it, the sensation travels through tiny tubes in dentin all the way to the nerve, causing sharp pain.

Sensitivity is essentially your tooth's way of telling you something is exposing the dentin. Finding and fixing the cause is the best long-term solution.

## Common Causes of Sensitive Teeth

Worn enamel from aggressive brushing is common. If you brush your teeth like you're scrubbing a floor, you're damaging your enamel and gums. Acid from acidic foods and beverages (soda, sports drinks, citrus, wine) slowly dissolves enamel. Stomach acid from reflux disease has the same effect.

Receding gums expose the root surface, which is softer than enamel and gets sensitive easily. Gum disease, aggressive brushing, or natural aging can cause recession. Teeth grinding (bruxism) wears away enamel. Clenching teeth creates stress that can break enamel. Cracked or chipped teeth expose dentin inside. Cavities expose dentin.

## Treating the Cause

Switching to a soft-bristled toothbrush and gentler brushing technique helps if aggressive brushing is your problem. Brush gently with small circular motions, not back-and-forth scrubbing.

If acidic foods and beverages are the problem, limit them or use a straw to bypass your teeth. After acidic exposure, rinse with water and wait 30 minutes before brushing (brushing immediately damages already-softened enamel). If you have stomach reflux, working with your doctor to control it protects your teeth from acid damage.

If you grind your teeth, ask your dentist about a night guard. This protects your enamel from grinding damage. If cracks or cavities are causing sensitivity, your dentist needs to treat them. A crack or cavity won't get better on its own—it'll get worse.

## Desensitizing Toothpaste

Desensitizing toothpaste contains compounds that block sensation travel through dentin tubes. It's very safe and often effective, especially for mild to moderate sensitivity. The compounds build up over weeks of use, so give it time—don't expect immediate relief.

Using desensitizing toothpaste twice daily provides better results than sporadic use. Let the toothpaste sit on sensitive areas for a few seconds before rinsing—this increases effectiveness. Some desensitizing toothpastes work better than others; if one doesn't help after a few weeks, try a different brand.

## Fluoride Treatments

Your dentist can apply concentrated fluoride gel or varnish to sensitive teeth. Fluoride strengthens enamel and helps block dentin tube sensation. Professional fluoride treatments are much stronger than over-the-counter products. One or a few treatments often significantly reduce sensitivity.

## Gum Grafting for Severe Recession

If sensitivity results from severe gum recession, grafting (surgically moving soft tissue to cover exposed root) might help. This is a bigger procedure than other treatments and doesn't suit everyone, but it's highly effective for severe cases where other treatments haven't helped.

## Root Canals as Last Resort

If sensitivity is severe and other treatments haven't helped, and if the sensitive tooth causes significant quality-of-life impact, root canal treatment is an option. This removes the nerve causing the sensitivity. However, it's aggressive—root canal is appropriate only after other treatments have failed.

## When Sensitivity Signals a Bigger Problem

Sensitivity in just one tooth, especially if it's severe or if you have pain when biting, might indicate a cracked tooth or cavity needing treatment. See your dentist to rule out these problems.

Sudden onset of severe sensitivity across multiple teeth, especially combined with other symptoms, might indicate gum disease needing professional treatment. Don't ignore unexplained widespread sensitivity.

## Prevention Strategies

Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and brush gently. Avoid very acidic foods and beverages, or use a straw when drinking them. If you have reflux, work with your doctor to control it. Wear a night guard if you grind your teeth. Address cavities and cracked teeth promptly. Maintain good gum health through proper brushing and flossing to prevent recession.

## Timeline for Improvement

Our [cavity prevention guide](/articles/risk-and-concerns-with-cavity-prevention-methods/) discusses overall tooth protection strategies. Over-the-counter desensitizing treatments typically take 2 to 3 weeks to show meaningful improvement. Professional treatments like fluoride applications sometimes help immediately or within days. Be patient—many solutions take time.

## Conclusion

Tooth sensitivity is usually fixable. Most causes are preventable with proper brushing, diet changes, and addressing underlying problems like grinding or reflux. Desensitizing toothpaste works for many people. Professional treatments like fluoride applications help others. Understanding your sensitivity's cause guides you toward the right solution. If home treatments don't help within several weeks, see your dentist to rule out more serious problems needing professional treatment.

## Key Takeaway

Tooth sensitivity results from exposed dentin usually caused by worn enamel or receded gums. Common causes include aggressive brushing, acidic foods, grinding, and gum disease. Many cases improve with desensitizing toothpaste or professional fluoride treatments. The best approach is fixing the underlying cause—gentler brushing, limiting acids, treating grinding. Severe sensitivity in one tooth or sudden widespread sensitivity needs professional evaluation to rule out cavities or gum disease.
