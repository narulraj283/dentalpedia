## Introduction and Classification of Tooth Fractures

Tooth fractures represent the second most common cause of tooth loss following caries and periodontal disease, affecting 10-25% of permanent dentitions during adult life. Fracture classification depends on anatomical extent: enamel-only fractures involve outer hard tissue without dentin exposure; enamel-dentin fractures expose the dentin layer but do not involve the pulp chamber; enamel-dentin-pulp fractures extend into the pulp tissue, necessitating endodontic intervention; and root fractures involve the root structure beneath the alveolar crest.

Clinical diagnosis requires visual examination under magnification, radiographic assessment to evaluate pulpal involvement and root continuity, and thermal sensitivity testing to assess pulp vitality. Pulp testing must occur within 30 minutes of trauma before transient ischemia resolves; testing performed later may yield false-negative responses. Documentation of baseline vitality status establishes baseline against which future color changes or response changes are compared.

## Enamel-Only Fractures: Conservative Management

Fractures limited to enamel without dentin involvement require primarily aesthetic and smoothing considerations. Intraoral examination under magnification and tactile probing identify any dentin exposure; if absent, restoration is elective rather than essential. However, unsupported enamel edges create sharp surfaces causing soft tissue trauma and psychological discomfort; most patients request treatment for comfort and appearance.

Smoothing sharp enamel margins by gentle polishing often suffices for small enamel chips <2 mm. Fluoride-releasing glass-ionomer materials may be applied to smooth surfaces lacking retention features, providing temporary smoothing while allowing enamel bonding in future years if patient desires. Resin-based composite bonding offers superior aesthetics for larger enamel fractures; contemporary adhesive systems provide long-term bond durability when applied to etched enamel surfaces.

Composite longevity for enamel-limited restorations averages 5-8 years; approximately 60% of restorations maintain clinical acceptability at 10-year follow-up. Marginal staining and slight shade drift represent most common reasons for replacement. Superior longevity occurs for bonded restorations with retention-enhancing cavity features created by minimal tooth preparation, compared to restorations relying solely on adhesive retention.

## Enamel-Dentin Fractures: Composite Restoration Techniques

Fractures exposing dentin require prompt restoration to prevent further dentin exposure and caries progression. Contemporary resin-based composite materials with modern adhesive systems provide superior aesthetic outcomes and acceptable longevity for anterior enamel-dentin fractures. Clinical success requires complete moisture control to prevent salivary contamination during restoration; absolute isolation via rubber dam represents gold standard, though alternative isolation techniques may be acceptable for posterior fractures.

Restoration technique involves etching exposed enamel margins (15-40% phosphoric acid, 15-30 seconds) followed by application of adhesive resin system according to manufacturer protocol. Direct composite resin buildup restores anatomical contours using layering techniques (opaque base for discolored dentin, dentin shade for mid-layers, enamel shade for external surface). Contemporary composite materials demonstrate compressive strength of 300-500 MPa, tensile strength of 40-80 MPa, and flexural strength of 80-150 MPa, comparable to restored tooth strength under most loading scenarios.

For anterior teeth, stratified composite techniques using different shade and translucency materials produce superior aesthetic results compared to monolithic restoration. Studies demonstrate that layered composite restorations exhibit superior colour match and natural appearance compared to restorations using single composite shade. Incisal edge mamelons may be recreated using grooved-incisal instruments that create subtle surface variations enhancing naturalistic appearance.

Composite restoration longevity for enamel-dentin anterior fractures averages 8-12 years, with approximately 70-75% of restorations maintaining clinical acceptability at 10-year follow-up. Fracture of the restoration itself represents the most common failure mode (25-30% of failures), followed by secondary caries at restoration margins (20-25%), and marginal discoloration or staining (15-20%). Factors promoting longevity include adequate cavity walls for composite retention, meticulous isolation preventing moisture contamination, proper condensation eliminating voids, and careful finishing/polishing creating smooth margins.

## Enamel-Dentin Fractures with Minimal Pulp Exposure

When fractures approach the pulp chamber without frank pulpal exposure (visualized as red/bleeding tissue), pulp capping techniques may preserve pulpal vitality. Calcium hydroxide, mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA), or biodentine represent materials demonstrating favorable biocompatibility and healing potential. Success rates for pulp capping range from 85-90% when applied to fresh fractures (within 24 hours) with complete moisture control, declining to 40-60% when applied more than 24 hours after trauma.

Proper pulp capping protocol involves gentle cleaning of the exposure site with sterile saline, hemostasis achievement if bleeding occurs, application of biocompatible capping material (calcium hydroxide 0.5-1.0 mm thickness or MTA 2-3 mm thickness), and definitive restoration completion. MTA demonstrates superior biocompatibility compared to traditional calcium hydroxide in contemporary studies, but both materials produce acceptable outcomes when applied appropriately. Biodentine, a newer substitute for calcium hydroxide exhibiting improved handling properties, shows promising initial results though longer-term comparative data remains limited.

Pulp vitality must be reassessed at 2-4 weeks post-treatment; if vitality remains present, definitive composite restoration completes treatment. If vitality testing becomes negative, endodontic treatment becomes necessary. Success rates are highest (85-90%) for pulp caps placed within 24 hours of trauma; delayed treatment significantly reduces success likelihood. For pediatric patients, pulp capping offers superior long-term benefits compared to primary endodontic treatment, preserving natural tooth function and reducing future endodontic complications.

## Extensive Enamel-Dentin-Pulp Fractures Requiring Endodontics

Fractures with obvious pulpal involvement necessitate endodontic treatment. The objective changes determining pulp status include visualization of red pulp tissue, frank hemorrhage, and positive vitality testing indicating vital but damaged pulp tissue. Management depends on clinical scenario: for teeth with complete root development, complete conventional endodontic therapy represents the treatment of choice; for teeth with incomplete root development in pediatric patients, revascularization procedures may be considered with specific indications.

Conventional root canal treatment removes necrotic and vital pulp tissue, followed by mechanical debridement and chemical disinfection of the root canal system. Contemporary techniques employ nickel-titanium rotary instruments facilitating more efficient canal shaping compared to stainless steel hand instruments. The goal is complete obturation of the root canal system with gutta-percha and root canal sealer, achieved through vertical or lateral condensation techniques.

Post-operative restoration requires attention to coronal seal adequacy; a 2-3 mm coronal gutta-percha layer should be maintained immediately apical to the structural restoration. Contemporary studies document that root canal-treated teeth survive longer when restored with complete-coverage crowns (success rate 85-92% at 10 years) compared to direct composite restoration alone (success rate 65-75% at 10 years). The higher survival rate reflects superior protection from fracture of weakened root-canal-treated teeth.

## Revascularization Procedures for Immature Permanent Teeth

Teeth with traumatic pulp necrosing while roots remain incompletely developed (apical foramina >1.1 mm diameter) present unique challenges. Traditional endodontic treatment in this scenario is suboptimal due to inability to achieve three-dimensional gutta-percha obturation in the divergent root canal of incompletely developed teeth. Regenerative endodontic procedures (revascularization) aim to initiate healing and continued root development.

Revascularization protocol involves complete chemomechanical decontamination of the root canal system, followed by intentional reintroduction of bacteria-free bleeding into the root canal system, and placement of a biocompatible coronal barrier (mineral trioxide aggregate or biodentine). The induced bleeding introduces pluripotent stem cells from the periapical region; in favorable cases, these cells differentiate and establish new blood supply, allowing continued root development.

Success of revascularization, defined as restoration of positive vitality responses and radiographic evidence of continued root development, occurs in approximately 60-75% of treated teeth in contemporary studies. Treatment success requires complete patient cooperation for extended recall protocol (monthly evaluation for 6 months, then quarterly for 12 months) and strict infection control. Revascularization remains the preferred approach for immature teeth with pulp necrosis when possible, substantially improving long-term prognosis compared to conventional apical plug techniques.

## Root Fractures and Surgical Considerations

Root fractures below the alveolar crest generally require orthodontic stabilization or surgical reduction to obtain functional healing. Intraoral films and 3D cone-beam imaging establish the fracture location (coronal, middle, or apical third); location substantially influences prognosis. Coronal-third fractures demonstrate the poorest prognosis due to increased mobility and coronal fragment devascularization risk; middle-third fractures show intermediate prognosis; apical-third fractures show most favorable healing potential.

Treatment involves repositioning fracture fragments into anatomical alignment, then stabilizing the tooth through flexible splinting. Splints may be adhesive composite-based (bonded to unaffected adjacent teeth) or arch wire-based; 4-week splint duration typically proves adequate. Vertical root fractures detected postoperatively may be managed through orthodontic extraction followed by implant replacement or accepting compromised tooth longevity.

## Long-Term Prognosis and Predictive Factors

Tooth fracture prognosis depends on multiple factors including fracture extent, timing of treatment, pulp involvement, extent of root development, and patient compliance. Teeth receiving prompt treatment within 24 hours demonstrate significantly better outcomes than delayed treatment. Endodontically treated teeth subsequently lose moisture from the root dentin, becoming more brittle and fracture-prone; approximately 15-20% of endodontically treated anterior teeth eventually fracture.

Coronal-coverage restoration of endodontically treated teeth substantially improves long-term success. A 15-year follow-up study of 342 endodontically treated teeth documented that 87% of teeth receiving crown restorations maintained vitality and attachment level, whereas only 64% of teeth restored with composite alone maintained health. This differential reflects superior fracture protection provided by full-coverage crowns.

## Conclusion

Tooth fracture management depends on fracture classification and extent of pulpal involvement. Enamel-only and uncomplicated enamel-dentin fractures respond favorably to composite restoration with longevity averaging 8-12 years. Extensive fractures with minor pulpal involvement may benefit from pulp capping procedures with success rates of 85-90% if applied within 24 hours. Irreversible pulp damage requires endodontic treatment, with subsequent crown restoration substantially improving long-term success. Root fractures require immediate stabilization and may benefit from surgical repositioning. Early diagnosis and prompt treatment substantially improve fracture outcomes across all classification categories.
