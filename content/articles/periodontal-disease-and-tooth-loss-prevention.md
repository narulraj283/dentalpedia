Periodontitis causes more tooth loss in adults than dental decay. Advanced gum disease destroys the bone supporting teeth, eventually leading to loosening and loss. However, aggressive treatment of even advanced periodontitis, combined with dedicated long-term management, can save teeth that would otherwise be lost.

## The Progression to Tooth Loss

As periodontitis progresses, bone loss accelerates. Teeth lose more of their supporting structure, becoming mobile (loose). Progressive bone loss eventually provides insufficient support for the tooth, resulting in tooth loss either through extraction or spontaneous loss.

The rate of progression varies dramatically based on disease severity, individual health factors, and treatment compliance. Some people lose teeth rapidly once periodontitis is advanced, while others with similar disease can maintain most teeth through aggressive management.

## Assessment of Tooth Retention Potential

When teeth become mobile from advanced periodontitis, periodontists assess retention potential—whether treatment can save the tooth or extraction is preferable. This assessment considers several factors.

The extent of bone loss is primary. If more than 50 percent of supporting bone is lost, prognosis becomes questionable. Teeth with remaining bone supporting the apical half (root tip) may be salvageable, while those with bone loss exceeding two-thirds are often unrestorable.

The specific pattern of bone loss matters. Vertical bone loss (affecting one or two tooth surfaces) may be manageable. Horizontal bone loss (affecting all surfaces equally) offers worse prognosis.

Root length and tooth size influence retention potential. Longer roots provide more surface area for bone reattachment. Multirooted teeth may have different bone loss on different roots, affecting overall prognosis.

## Extraction as an Option

For teeth with poor retention potential, extraction may be the most practical approach. Maintaining mobile teeth requires intensive treatment with uncertain outcomes, while extraction removes the source of ongoing inflammation and infection.

For some patients, accepting tooth loss and planning replacement through implants or fixed bridges offers better long-term outcomes than attempting to save severely compromised teeth. This is a personal decision involving discussion with your dentist about realistic expectations for specific teeth.

## Splinting Stabilization

For mobile teeth with reasonable retention potential, splinting (bonding adjacent teeth together) can stabilize them. This distributes chewing forces across multiple teeth, reducing stress on individual compromised teeth.

Splinting is temporary in some cases—hopefully allowing bone regeneration through aggressive treatment. In other cases, splinting may be permanent, providing long-term stability for teeth that would otherwise be mobile and uncomfortable.

## Surgical Treatment for Advanced Disease

Surgical approaches for advanced periodontitis aim to gain access to deep pockets and severely diseased root surfaces that cannot be adequately treated non-surgically.

Periodontal flap surgery involves reflecting the gum flap to directly visualize the bone and root surfaces. This allows thorough removal of calculus and diseased tissue, smoothing of root surfaces, and assessment of bone anatomy for potential regeneration.

## Bone Grafting and Regeneration Techniques

For specific patterns of bone loss, bone grafting can restore some lost bone. Autogenous bone (bone from the patient's own body) provides the best results, though harvesting requires additional surgery.

Allogeneic bone (processed cadaver bone) and xenogeneic bone (bone-like material from animals) provide alternatives avoiding donor site morbidity. These materials work best combined with barrier membranes and growth factors.

Regeneration is never complete—generally, procedures regenerate 50 to 70 percent of lost bone in best-case scenarios. However, this may be sufficient to stabilize previously mobile teeth.

## Guided Tissue Regeneration

Barrier membranes placed during flap surgery create space allowing periodontal ligament and bone cells to repopulate healing sites, while excluding epithelial cells that block regeneration.

When combined with growth factors and bone grafting materials, GTR enhances regenerative outcomes. However, this approach is more complex and costly than conventional flap surgery.

## Managing Aggressive Periodontitis

Some patients have aggressive forms of periodontitis affecting younger patients or progressing rapidly despite reasonable home care. These forms may involve specific bacterial pathogens or immune system compromise.

Aggressive periodontitis may require additional treatment including antimicrobial therapy (specific antibiotics targeting pathogenic bacteria) and more intensive monitoring and maintenance intervals.

Genetic testing can sometimes identify aggressive disease predisposition, allowing more aggressive prevention and earlier intervention.

## Long-Term Maintenance for Salvaged Teeth

Once teeth have been saved through aggressive treatment of advanced periodontitis, long-term commitment to maintenance is absolutely critical. Lapse in maintenance typically results in disease recurrence and eventual loss of previously salvaged teeth.

Maintenance visits every two to three months (rather than standard six-month intervals) are typically necessary. Meticulous daily home care is essential.

## When Extraction Is Appropriate

For severely diseased teeth with poor prognosis despite aggressive treatment, extraction may be the most prudent choice. Tooth extraction removes the source of infection and inflammation, which may benefit overall health and periodontal health of remaining teeth.

Replacement through implants or bridges restores function and esthetics. Modern replacement options can provide excellent results—sometimes better than attempting to maintain severely compromised natural teeth.

## Cost-Benefit Considerations

Advanced periodontitis treatment is expensive and time-intensive. Multiple surgical appointments, specialized testing, frequent maintenance visits, and potentially long-term splinting or bone grafting require significant investment.

Comparing this cost and effort to extraction and replacement helps guide appropriate decision-making. For some teeth, investment in aggressive treatment offers good value. For others, extraction and replacement is more practical.

## Prevention: The Real Goal

The best approach to preventing tooth loss from periodontitis is prevention of periodontitis itself. Excellent daily home care, regular professional monitoring, and addressing risk factors prevent most gum disease.

For those with periodontitis, early intervention—before advanced bone loss occurs—saves teeth far more reliably than attempting to salvage teeth when disease is far advanced.

Even advanced periodontitis can sometimes be managed to prevent tooth loss, but success requires aggressive professional treatment, substantial patient compliance, and often significant cost and effort. Prevention through good home care and early treatment remains far superior to attempting to save teeth threatened by advanced disease.
