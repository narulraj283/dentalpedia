## Neurobiological Mechanisms of Anxiety-Driven Bruxism

Stress-related bruxism represents a complex neuromuscular response modulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. When psychological stressors activate the amygdala, corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) triggers a cascade that elevates cortisol and epinephrine levels, increasing sympathetic nervous system activity. This heightened state of arousal directly influences the masticatory motor centers in the brainstem, specifically the trigeminal motor nucleus, which becomes hyperexcitable during periods of anxiety. The relationship between anxiety disorders and bruxism is well-established: patients with generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and major depressive disorder report grinding frequency 2-3 times higher than non-anxious controls. The temporal relationship is particularly striking during REM sleep onset periods when muscle atonia-resistance mechanisms are activated, creating conditions for forceful jaw clenching with bite forces reaching 300-800 Newtons—far exceeding normal mastication (70-120 Newtons).

Neurochemical dysregulation in anxiety-driven bruxism involves serotonergic and dopaminergic pathways. Low serotonin levels correlate with increased bruxism severity, explaining why selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) can paradoxically worsen grinding in some patients during initial treatment phases, though long-term management typically improves outcomes. The orexin system also plays a critical role; orexin-A neurons in the lateral hypothalamus regulate sleep-wake transitions and muscle tone regulation. Dysregulation in this system during anxiety manifests as microarousals that trigger rhythmic masticatory muscle activity (RMMA) in the sleep architecture.

## Sleep Bruxism versus Awake Bruxism

Clinically distinguishing sleep bruxism from awake bruxism is essential for targeted management. Sleep bruxism occurs during sleep stages 1-2, during K-complexes and sleep spindles that represent cortical microarousals, with episode duration of 5-30 seconds and frequencies of 6-20 episodes per hour of sleep. The grinding sounds (audible bruxism) result from lateral jaw movements reaching 10-15 millimeters with anterolateral trajectories. Polysomnographic recordings reveal characteristic electromyographic (EMG) patterns: a 2-second silent period followed by intense bilateral masseter and temporalis contraction in bursts lasting 1-2 seconds, often coinciding with respiratory microarousals.

Awake bruxism differs significantly—it is a sustained, often unconscious clenching rather than grinding, occurring during stressful situations or intense concentration with bite forces sustained over minutes to hours. Awake bruxism shows no diurnal pattern and demonstrates stronger correlation with anxiety, depression, and workplace stress. Awake clenching produces predominantly vertical forces, causing concentric wear patterns on occlusal surfaces, while sleep bruxism produces lateral faceting on cusps and ridges. Distinguishing these phenotypes is critical because treatment protocols diverge: sleep bruxism may require polysomnographic evaluation and possible pharmacotherapy, while awake bruxism responds better to behavioral modification and stress management.

## Polysomnographic Assessment and Diagnostic Criteria

Full-night polysomnography (PSG) combined with audio-video recording represents the gold standard for sleep bruxism diagnosis. The International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD-3) diagnostic criteria require observation of either audible grinding or rhythmic masticatory muscle activity documented on EMG, occurring at ≥4 episodes per hour of sleep (or ≥6 per night), with additional criterion of grinding sounds or dental wear damage. PSG involves bilateral surface EMG electrode placement over the anterior temporalis and masseter muscles, sampled at 100-200 Hz minimum, with simultaneous electroencephalography (EEG), electrooculography (EOG), and submental electromyography to distinguish sleep stages.

The EMG activity pattern during bruxism episodes shows characteristic "phasic" bursts (amplitude >10% of maximum voluntary contraction) occurring in clusters. Polysomnographic severity grading includes: mild bruxism (4-8 episodes/hour), moderate (8-15 episodes/hour), and severe (>15 episodes/hour). Associated respiratory parameters should be assessed—bruxism episodes frequently coincide with oxygen desaturation events and respiratory arousal, particularly in patients with comorbid sleep-disordered breathing. Home sleep testing devices with portable EMG capabilities offer lower-cost screening, though they lack full PSG sensitivity for diagnosing mild cases. Questionnaire-based screening using the Bruxism Severity Index (BSI) or Jaw Functional Limitation Scale (JFLS) can stratify patients for referral, though these instruments alone have insufficient diagnostic specificity for bruxism versus other TMJ disorders.

## Stress Physiology and Muscle Hyperactivity

The stress response in bruxism involves sustained elevation of muscle tone through multiple mechanisms. During wakefulness, the prefrontal cortex typically maintains inhibitory control over lower brainstem motor nuclei through gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) and glutamate balance. Anxiety disorders involve reduced prefrontal-limbic connectivity, impairing this inhibitory control. Simultaneously, noradrenergic projection from the locus coeruleus to masticatory motor centers increases baseline excitability. This results in resting masseter and temporalis EMG activity 2-3 times higher in anxious individuals, and kinesiographic studies show increased jaw clenching frequency during stress tasks.

The temporal relationship between stress exposure and bruxism onset is particularly evident in examining occupational or academic stressors. Dental students, physicians, and attorneys report a 40-60% prevalence of stress-related bruxism during high-demand periods. The chronic elevation of cortisol dysregulates the normal diurnal cortisol curve, impacting sleep quality and increasing vulnerability to sleep microarousals that trigger RMMA. Furthermore, chronic stress depletes serotonin stores while upregulating excitatory glutamatergic neurotransmission, creating a biochemical environment favoring bruxism.

## Patient Screening and Anxiety Assessment

Comprehensive screening for anxiety-driven bruxism requires multifaceted assessment. Clinical examination should assess for specific wear patterns: 1) cusp tip flattening on posterior teeth (bruxism facets), 2) incisal edge wear with flat surfaces on anterior teeth, 3) abraded material at the gingival third of facial surfaces (tension-induced attrition), and 4) internal stress lines visible on radiographs indicating repetitive loading. Dental mobility evaluation using a periodontal probe to apply 0.2 kg force can reveal increased tooth mobility (>1 mm) in anterior teeth—common in chronic bruxers due to combined occlusal trauma and periodontal microtrauma.

Psychological screening should incorporate validated anxiety instruments: the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale (GAD-7) with cutoff ≥10 indicating moderate anxiety, the Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS) specifically designed for medical populations, or the Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) with established norms. Patient history should detail sleep characteristics: sleep onset latency, nighttime awakenings, unrefreshed sleep sensation, morning jaw pain (present in 60% of sleep bruxers), and awareness of grinding sounds (spousal reports are valuable). Quantifying stress exposure through occupational assessment and life-event inventories provides context for targeted intervention.

Validated screening instruments specific to bruxism include the Bruxism Screening Questionnaire developed by Lobbezoo et al., which incorporates questions about grinding sounds, morning jaw symptoms, teeth clenching awareness, and stress levels. A positive screening warrants referral for portable sleep EMG or full PSG if moderate-severe bruxism is suspected based on clinical findings.

## Occlusal Guard Design and Materials

Occlusal guards serve both mechanical protection and neuromuscular modification functions in bruxism management. Hard acrylic guards (2-3 mm thickness) manufactured from polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) provide superior durability and wear detection compared to soft guards; they create a "detection surface" where wear patterns become visible, serving as biofeedback mechanism. Hard guard thickness should be individually calculated: guards <1.5 mm offer inadequate force distribution (resulting in >200 MPa contact stresses that can damage residual natural tooth structure), while those >4 mm create unfavorable changes to condylar position and jaw mechanics. The optimal range is 2.0-3.0 mm.

Design considerations include:

**Coverage**: Full palatal coverage provides superior force distribution and prevents midline splitting that occurs in palatal-half designs. Bilateral coverage of posterior teeth is essential—unilateral guards create asymmetric loading patterns.

**Occlusal Surface Anatomy**: Flat or slightly curved surfaces (avoiding sharp cuspal features) allow muscle relaxation by reducing proprioceptive input that triggers bruxism. Some designs incorporate lateral guidance canines or working-side contacts to distribute anteroposterior forces, reducing masseter loading.

**Incisal Guidance**: A shallow anterior guidance angle (20-25 degrees) can reduce posterior tooth forces during lateral movements by approximately 30%, decreasing nocturnal muscle activity in some patients.

**Vertical Dimension**: Guards fabricated at the patient's centric relation with vertical dimension equivalent to the patient's normal resting facial height avoid creating unfavorable condylar-disk relationships. Increasing VDO by >2 mm risks exacerbating clicking or joint symptoms.

Material selection beyond PMMA includes thermoplastic copolyester (flexible, less durable), latex-free rubber (moderate durability, better proprioceptive feedback), and laboratory-processed guards versus chair-side fabricated. Hybrid designs combining hard acrylic with soft borders offer force distribution benefits. Replacement intervals depend on wear severity; severe bruxers require replacement every 6-12 months, while mild cases may extend to 24 months. Night guard compliance is critical—40-50% of patients abandon guards within 6 months due to comfort issues, emphasizing the need for patient education and adjustment visits.

## Anxiolytic Treatment Approaches

Pharmacological management of anxiety-driven bruxism requires careful consideration of drug effects on bruxism severity. Benzodiazepines, particularly clonazepam (0.5-2.0 mg nocturnal dosing), reduce bruxism through GABA-A receptor agonism increasing inhibitory neurotransmission. Studies demonstrate 40-60% reduction in RMMA episodes, though chronic use carries addiction risk and tolerance development, limiting long-term utility. Buspirone (15-45 mg daily), a 5-HT1A partial agonist with anxiolytic properties and minimal motor side effects, shows moderate bruxism reduction without dependency potential.

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors warrant caution—initial SSRI therapy (first 2-6 weeks) may worsen bruxism through serotonin-mediated effects on motor neurons, though long-term SSRI use typically improves outcomes as anxiety remission develops. If bruxism worsens with SSRI initiation, switching to serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) like venlafaxine or adding augmentation agents (bupropion, buspirone, betahistine) can mitigate the effect.

Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline 10-50 mg nocturnal dosing) possess anticholinergic and noradrenergic properties that reduce muscle hyperactivity. Tricyclic use requires cardiac and ophthalmologic screening given anticholinergic side effects (dry mouth, urinary retention).

Non-pharmacological approaches include cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) specifically targeting stress response and anxiety, with meta-analyses showing 30-40% symptom reduction. Progressive muscle relaxation, mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), and sleep hygiene optimization (consistent sleep schedule, cool dark bedroom, avoiding caffeine after 2 PM) provide evidence-based adjuncts. Biofeedback using portable jaw-tracking devices that alert patients to clenching episodes during wakefulness offers targeted behavioral modification, particularly for awake bruxism.

## Clinical Management Protocol

Comprehensive bruxism management requires a stepwise, individually-tailored approach:

**Phase 1** (Initial Assessment): Clinical examination for wear patterns and dental sequelae, questionnaire-based anxiety screening (GAD-7), sleep history documentation, and discussion of symptoms and risk factors.

**Phase 2** (Diagnostic Confirmation): Portable EMG or full polysomnography for suspected sleep bruxism with clinical or radiographic severity indicators.

**Phase 3** (Intervention Initiation):
- Occlusal guard fabrication with bilateral coverage, 2.0-3.0 mm thickness, and patient adjustment appointments at 1-week and 1-month intervals
- Referral for anxiety assessment and treatment initiation if GAD-7 ≥10 or if clinical judgment indicates significant psychosocial stress
- Sleep hygiene counseling and stress management education

**Phase 4** (Monitoring and Adjustment): Three-month follow-up assessing guard wear, symptom reduction, compliance, and need for occlusal adjustments. Reassessment of anxiety symptoms and pharmacotherapy efficacy.

**Phase 5** (Long-term Management): Annual dental examination monitoring for new wear patterns, tooth mobility progression, or periodontal deterioration. Reassess anxiety status and medication side effects. Upgrade guard if wear patterns indicate mechanical failure.

Patients should understand that bruxism management is chronic—complete cessation is achievable in only 20-30% of cases, while 50-70% experience symptom reduction with multimodal intervention. Prognosis is better for awake bruxism with anxiety-focused treatment, and for sleep bruxism when underlying sleep disorders are concurrently managed.

## Conclusion

Stress-related bruxism represents a treatable condition when approached through understanding of underlying neuroendocrine dysregulation, accurate phenotyping (sleep versus awake), and coordinated management combining dental protection, anxiety treatment, and sleep optimization. Clinicians should maintain awareness of HPA axis physiology, recommend appropriate polysomnographic assessment when indicated, and collaborate with mental health providers for optimal patient outcomes.
