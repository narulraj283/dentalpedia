# Root Caries - Decay on Exposed Root Surfaces

Root surface caries represents a distinct disease process affecting exposed cementum and dentin, increasingly prevalent in aging populations where periodontal recession and reduced salivary function create permissive environments. Unlike coronal caries occurring on enamel, root caries involves structurally vulnerable cementum—thinner, more easily demineralized, and less resistant to microbial invasion. This comprehensive review examines pathogenic mechanisms, epidemiological patterns, diagnostic approaches, and evidence-based prevention and restoration strategies essential for managing high-risk populations.

## Pathophysiology of Root Surface Caries

Root caries develops through classical caries pathogenesis—the interaction of susceptible substrate (cementum/dentin), cariogenic microbiota, dietary fermentable carbohydrates, and time. Cementum differs fundamentally from enamel: it lacks crystalline organization, contains approximately 50% inorganic mineral compared to 96% in enamel, and exhibits greater porosity and permeability. These structural characteristics render cementum 4-10 times more susceptible to demineralization than enamel, requiring only modest acidification (pH <6.8) to initiate carious dissolution.

The root surface caries lesion typically initiates beneath the gingival margin following gingival recession, creating an anaerobic microenvironment favorable for acidogenic microorganisms. Early lesions appear as small, discreet areas of discoloration on the cementum surface—typically brown, dark brown, or even black—distinct from the grayish appearance of coronal enamel caries. Progressive lesions become slightly depressed, soften with explorer pressure, and gradually extend proximally and subgingivally. The lesion architecture differs from coronal caries: root caries spreads laterally along the cemento-dentin junction beneath an intact but demineralized superficial layer, creating an undermined appearance on cross-section.

Saliva plays a pivotal protective role through buffering capacity, antimicrobial proteins, and remineralization potential. Individuals with compromised salivary flow (xerostomia from medication, systemic disease, or radiation) experience dramatically elevated root caries risk—studies demonstrate 5-10 times higher incidence in those with salivary flow <0.5mL/minute compared to normal-flow individuals. The specific microbial ecology of root surface caries involves acidogenic species including Streptococcus mutans, Lactobacillus acidophilus, and increasingly recognized Scardovia wiggsiae, which demonstrates exceptional cariogenicity on root surfaces.

## Epidemiology and Risk Assessment

Root caries prevalence increases dramatically with age, affecting 10-17% of dentate older adults in developed nations and proportions exceeding 40% in certain institutional populations. However, not all older adults develop root caries—rather, specific risk factors stratify populations into high and low-risk groups. Uncontrolled dietary sucrose frequency (>3 times daily between meals) represents the strongest behavioral risk indicator, while clinical factors including exposed root surfaces, existing root restorations, and periodontal disease history constitute significant structural risk predictors.

Medical comorbidities substantially increase root caries risk: diabetes increases incidence by 2-3 fold through reduced salivary flow and impaired immune function; medications causing xerostomia (anticholinergics, antihistamines, SSRIs) increase risk proportionally to flow reduction; head and neck radiation causes permanent salivary gland damage predisposing to rampant caries. Functional limitations in older adults (arthritis, cognitive decline, tremor) compromise oral hygiene effectiveness, further elevating risk. Socioeconomic factors including limited dental access, reduced oral health literacy, and financial constraints restrict preventive interventions.

Assessment tools incorporating multiple risk factors enhance identification of susceptible individuals. The American Dental Association's Caries Risk Assessment Tool categorizes patients into low, moderate, or high risk based on clinical findings and behavioral factors. High-risk patients require intensive prevention programs, frequent monitoring (3-4 month intervals), and aggressive intervention at earliest lesion detection. This risk-stratified approach allows resource optimization while ensuring vulnerable populations receive appropriate intensity of care.

## Clinical Diagnosis and Lesion Activity Assessment

Root caries diagnosis combines visual examination, tactile exploration, and often radiographic confirmation, though early lesions frequently escape detection without systematic screening. Visual examination in good lighting reveals discoloration changes on root surfaces visible in the gingival third of the tooth. Early lesions appear as subtle brown or reddish discoloration; more advanced lesions develop distinctive cavitation with softened margins. The lesion border frequently shows distinct demarcation from sound root structure, though this margin may be obscured by supragingival plaque or calculus.

Tactile assessment using light explorer pressure (not the firm pressure used for coronal caries diagnosis, which may cause unnecessary damage) provides supplementary information. Active lesions typically feel sticky or soft with the explorer; arrested lesions feel harder and may be darkly stained. However, tactile assessment alone proves unreliable and may introduce false positives—visual criteria correlated with activity assessment should guide diagnosis. Radiographic visualization of root caries proves challenging due to the oblique anatomy of root surfaces and radiographic technique limitations; lesions may not appear radiographically until substantial demineralization occurs. Therefore, visual assessment coupled with clinical risk evaluation provides superior diagnostic accuracy.

Activity assessment distinguishes active (progressing) from arrested (inactive) lesions, which influences management urgency and intervention approach. Active lesions exhibit specific characteristics: plaque coverage, location at the gingival margin, light color (pale yellow or tan), and soft consistency. Arrested lesions appear darkly stained, isolated from the gingival margin, harder texture, and lack surrounding plaque biofilm. Two-toned lesions—displaying both active (lighter) and arrested (dark) portions—indicate mixed activity status. Quantitative approaches using standardized photography and digital analysis enable objective activity assessment and lesion progression monitoring in clinical trials and high-risk patient populations.

## Prevention Through Saliva Management and Dietary Modification

Saliva optimization represents the cornerstone of root caries prevention, particularly for individuals with compromised salivary flow. Xerostomia management includes modification of medications (if medically feasible), topical saliva substitutes, and stimulants including xylitol-containing products, sugar-free gum, and prescription pilocarpine. Saliva substitutes based on carboxymethyl cellulose or xanthan gum provide temporary lubrication and pH buffering but lack the antimicrobial and remineralization properties of natural saliva. Saliva stimulation through mechanical means (sugar-free gum or xylitol lozenges) or pharmacological agents (pilocarpine) increases endogenous saliva flow by 50-100%, providing superior protective effects compared to substitutes alone.

Dietary counseling targeting reduction of dietary acid exposure and fermentable carbohydrate frequency proves essential. Patients should limit between-meal snacking, avoid sipping acidic beverages (citrus juice, cola, wine) without protective strategies, and reduce sucrose frequency to ≤3 occasions daily. Sugar-free alternatives including xylitol-sweetened products provide sweetness without cariogenic potential; moreover, xylitol demonstrates antimicrobial activity against cariogenic species and enhances remineralization. Recommendation of water as the primary beverage, restriction of sticky carbohydrate foods, and consumption of protective foods (cheese, milk products) containing calcium and phosphate complement mechanical prevention strategies.

Fluoride utilization remains essential in root caries prevention, with concentration-dependent efficacy demonstrated across multiple studies. Neutral sodium fluoride 1.1% rinse (daily) or 2% gel (weekly) demonstrates superior effectiveness compared to acidulated phosphate fluoride formulations, which may cause additional cementum demineralization in susceptible individuals. For severe xerostomia or high-risk populations, custom-fitted trays delivering 1.1% neutral fluoride gel daily provide maximal local delivery. Patient compliance substantially affects outcomes—daily fluoride use reduces root caries incidence by 40-60% compared to no fluoride. Combination approaches integrating dietary modification, enhanced home care, and professional fluoride application achieve superior outcomes compared to single interventions.

## Silver Diamine Fluoride: Paradigm Shift in Root Caries Treatment

Silver diamine fluoride (SDF) represents a paradigm shift in root caries management, offering noninvasive treatment of active lesions with antimicrobial, remineralization, and arresting properties superior to fluoride alone. The 38% SDF solution (Riva Star, Advantage Arrest) contains silver nitrate and ammonium fluoride, delivering dual antimicrobial silver ions and fluoride for remineralization. Clinical application involves cotton roll placement, lesion isolation, and 1-minute topical application with microbrush contact. The application produces rapid color change from light brown to black/gray through reaction of silver with organic matter and sulfur-containing proteins—this color change confirms lesion penetration and indicates arrest/arrest.

The antimicrobial mechanism operates through silver ions disrupting bacterial cell wall synthesis and inhibiting cellular respiration, achieving bacterial reduction exceeding 99% in vitro and clinically eliminating cariogenic microorganisms. Fluoride ions promote remineralization through hydroxyapatite crystal formation on demineralized substrate, converting vulnerable cementum to a highly remineralized surface resistant to subsequent caries development. Clinical studies demonstrate 80-90% arrest rates of active root lesions following single SDF application, with sustained arrest at 3-year follow-up. The arrested lesion surface hardness increases 2-3 fold compared to baseline, improving resistance to mechanical challenge.

Limitations include esthetic concerns—the blackish discoloration persists on visible surfaces and concerns some patients, though the lesion itself becomes nonprogressive. Potential argyria (permanent grayish skin discoloration) from systemic silver absorption remains theoretically possible but unreported in clinical practice at recommended concentrations. Allergy to silver or fluoride represents an absolute contraindication; pregnancy (trimester-specific concern for fluoride) and young children <3 years require cautious application. SDF proves particularly advantageous for functionally compromised elderly patients unable to tolerate restoration placement, frail nursing home residents, and individuals declining invasive procedures. Integration of SDF into prevention protocols for high-risk populations represents a significant advancement in nonsurgical caries management.

## Glass Ionomer Cement Restoration

Glass ionomer cement (GIC) restoratives represent the preferred material for root caries lesions, offering superior adhesion, fluoride release, and biocompatibility compared to resin composites. The acid-base polymerization reaction creates a silicate matrix with incorporated fluoroaluminosilicate glass particles, releasing fluoride progressively over years and continuously recharging when exposed to topical fluoride. This characteristic provides dual benefits: initial high fluoride release during the critical healing phase and sustained antimicrobial activity and remineralization during long-term service.

Conventional GIC demonstrates excellent retention on cementum and dentin, with micromechanical interlocking enhanced by the acidic etching effect inherent to the cement system. Resin-modified GIC adds resin components, improving early strength and reducing sensitivity to moisture contamination during placement. High-viscosity GIC formulations (such as those used in ART—atraumatic restorative treatment) provide superior compressive strength and resistance to attrition. The alkaline environment created by GIC placement raises local pH, inhibiting cariogenic bacteria and promoting demineralization reversal at the lesion-restoration interface.

Restoration placement requires thorough removal of soft, decomposed cement and dentin while preserving hard, remineralized substrate. Selective caries excavation using hand instruments or rotary burs at slow speed removes easily dislodged carious tissue but stops when hard dentin resistance is encountered. Overexcavation unnecessarily removes sound substrate and increases restoration bulk requirements. Following isolation with rubber dam or cotton rolls, place GIC according to manufacturer instructions, applying moisturizing layers for conventional GIC or light-polymerizing units for resin-modified formulations. GIC resorption from saliva exposure necessitates complete gingival coverage and moisture protection during the initial setting period.

Clinical outcomes demonstrate GIC retention exceeding 90% at 3-year follow-up with minimal secondary caries development. Longevity correlates with patient compliance with fluoride application—those receiving regular professional or home fluoride application maintain restorations without secondary caries, while those without fluoride supplementation show higher failure rates. GIC wear requires periodic replacement but occurs at rates similar to or slower than coronal restorations in high-caries-risk populations.

## Prevention in Institutional Settings

Nursing home and long-term care populations experience extreme root caries prevalence—studies report 40-70% of residents with untreated root caries, reflecting limited dental access, caregiver knowledge gaps, and functional limitations preventing self-care. Systemic implementation of prevention protocols including supervised fluoride application, dietary counseling, and enhanced caregiver education significantly reduces incidence and disease progression. Establishment of quarterly dental screening programs identifying high-risk residents enables early intervention before extensive disease develops.

Dietary modification proves particularly challenging in institutional settings, where centralized food preparation and resident autonomy issues complicate sucrose reduction. Implementation of low-sucrose diets, elimination of frequent snacking, and education of dietary staff regarding caries prevention enhance outcomes. Caregiver training in assisted oral hygiene—proper toothbrushing technique, floss or interdental device use, and fluoride application—improves individual plaque control when residents cannot independently maintain hygiene. Professional topical fluoride application at 3-month intervals provides chemical prevention for those unable to independently use fluoride products.

SDF application in institutional settings offers particular advantages: rapid application (1 minute per tooth), no restoration requirements, excellent arrest efficacy, and reduced need for invasive procedures. Its integration into institutional dental protocols improves care quality for vulnerable populations with limited treatment options. Regular monitoring of treated lesions confirms arrest and guides retreat plans for lesions showing activity despite initial intervention.

## Long-Term Management and Patient Education

Root caries management extends beyond immediate intervention to encompass long-term monitoring and behavioral modification. Patient education addressing the specific disease mechanisms—cementum vulnerability, microbial pathogenesis, dietary effects—increases comprehension and motivation for compliance. Clear explanation that root caries differs from coronal caries and requires distinctive prevention strategies acknowledges the distinct disease entity and justifies specific recommendations.

Individualized prevention protocols based on specific risk factors prove superior to generic recommendations. High-risk patients require documentation and communication of risk status, quarterly monitoring appointments, regular professional fluoride application, and dietary assessment with specific modification targets. Moderate-risk patients benefit from 6-month intervals and annual fluoride application. Low-risk patients may be managed through standard preventive intervals with patient-directed prevention.

Documentation of root caries lesions using standardized photography enables objective progression assessment at follow-up visits. Serial imaging provides tangible feedback to patients regarding arrest or progression, enhancing motivation for behavior change. Integration of root caries risk assessment into comprehensive preventive protocols ensures that individuals with clinical and behavioral indicators of vulnerability receive appropriate intensity of intervention. Success in root caries prevention requires sustained effort across multiple visit intervals and coordination between patient compliance and professional intervention intensity.
