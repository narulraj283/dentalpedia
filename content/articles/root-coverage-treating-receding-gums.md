## What Is Gum Recession and Why Should You Care?

Your gums are supposed to cover your tooth roots completely. When your gums recede (move down), they expose the root surface. This creates cosmetic concerns—your tooth looks longer and your smile looks different. More importantly, exposed roots are more vulnerable to decay and sensitivity because roots lack the protective enamel that covers the crown of your tooth.

Gum recession affects about 15-30% of the population at some level. It can be mild (you might not even notice), or severe (with significant root exposure). Sometimes it's just one tooth; sometimes multiple teeth are affected. Understanding why it happens and what you can do about it helps you decide if treatment is right for you.

## Why Your Gums Are Receding

Aggressive brushing is one common cause. Brushing too hard or using a hard-bristled toothbrush traumatizes your gums over time, causing them to recede. Switching to a soft-bristled brush and gentler technique can prevent continued recession. Your dentist can show you proper technique if you're unsure.

Gum disease causes recession as the infection damages the attachment between your gums and teeth. Smoking increases gum disease risk and recession risk substantially. If you smoke, quitting helps prevent further recession. Hormonal changes during pregnancy, menopause, or other times can make gums more vulnerable.

Braces or orthodontic movement can cause recession if teeth are moved too far buccally (toward the cheek). Some people's gum tissue is naturally thin or their teeth are positioned in a way that makes recession more likely. Genetics and tooth position play roles you can't control.

Severe gum recession from deep periodontal pockets indicates gum disease requiring treatment. If you notice recession or your dentist mentions it, this is a sign to improve your oral hygiene and possibly get professional gum disease treatment.

## What Root Coverage Treatment Involves

If your recession is mild and not progressing, you might not need treatment. Monitoring your gums and preventing further recession through better oral care might be all that's necessary. Your dentist can track whether recession is getting worse by measuring it at your appointments.

For significant recession causing cosmetic or functional concerns, grafting procedures can cover exposed roots. The most successful technique, subepithelial connective tissue grafting, takes tissue from your palate and places it over the exposed root. Your own tissue works beautifully and integrates well.

Another technique, the coronally advanced flap, repositions your existing gum tissue over the exposed root. This works well when you have adequate gum tissue available to reposition. The advantage is that no tissue is taken from another area, so there's only one surgical site.

## Success Rates and Expectations

Properly done graft procedures achieve 85-100% root coverage in favorable situations. Your specific outcome depends on factors like the amount of recession, the size and location of the exposed root, whether multiple teeth are affected, and your healing response.

Expect some healing time. After grafting, you'll need to be very careful with the area—no brushing in the grafted zone for several weeks, gentle rinsing, soft foods. Complete healing takes 3-6 months. The graft tissue continues remodeling for up to a year, with the final appearance settling in around 6-12 months.

You'll likely notice improved esthetics—the tooth looks shorter and your smile looks more balanced. Root sensitivity usually improves dramatically. Exposed roots are very sensitive to cold and sometimes to touch; coverage eliminates this sensitivity for most people.

## Choosing Your Treatment

Mild recession without symptoms might not need treatment. Many people live comfortably with minor recession. If it bothers you cosmetically or if sensitivity is affecting your quality of life, treatment is worth considering.

Your dentist will assess your specific situation. They'll measure recession severity, evaluate your remaining gum tissue, and predict what coverage percentage is realistically achievable. They'll discuss timeline (the procedure takes 1-2 hours, but full healing takes months), cost, and maintenance.

Smoking dramatically reduces graft success. If you smoke, quitting significantly improves your chances of successful coverage. Excellent oral hygiene supports healing. Poor oral care increases graft failure risk.

## Preventing Recession From Happening Again

Once your gums are treated, preventing new recession is crucial. Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and brush gently—don't scrub hard. Water flossers are gentler than string floss for sensitive areas. Avoid aggressive tooth picking or manipulation of your gums.

If gum disease is the underlying issue, you need periodontal treatment and excellent home care to stop disease progression. Your dentist or periodontal specialist can guide you. Smoking cessation is one of the highest-impact changes you can make.

Protect your teeth from aggressive orthodontics or tooth grinding. Wear a night guard if you grind your teeth. Ensure your bite is balanced so one area isn't receiving excessive force.

## Conclusion

Gum recession is common and treatable. Whether you need treatment depends on severity, symptoms, and how much it bothers you. Modern grafting procedures achieve excellent coverage results in most cases, restoring both function and appearance. Preventing further recession through gentle oral care and addressing underlying causes like gum disease is your best long-term strategy.

## Key Takeaway

Gum recession can be surgically corrected with grafting procedures achieving 85-100% root coverage, but prevention through gentle oral care and treating gum disease is better than needing treatment.
