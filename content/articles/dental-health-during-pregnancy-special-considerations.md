## Why Dental Health Matters During Pregnancy

Pregnancy creates unique oral health challenges due to hormonal changes, dietary shifts, and physical changes. Poor oral health during pregnancy increases risks of pregnancy complications including gestational diabetes and premature birth. Additionally, babies born to mothers with untreated gum disease are at increased risk for low birth weight and premature delivery.

Hormonal changes during pregnancy increase gum inflammation and bleeding. Even with excellent oral hygiene, many pregnant women develop gingivitis. Additionally, pregnancy hormones increase cavity risk, as does any dietary shift toward more frequent snacking.

## Hormonal Effects on Oral Health

Increased progesterone and estrogen levels affect gum tissue response to plaque, increasing inflammation and bleeding. This pregnancy gingivitis typically develops during the second trimester and worsens if not managed. Women who already have gum disease experience more severe inflammation during pregnancy.

Some pregnant women develop pregnancy tumors (epulis), which are benign gum growths resulting from hormonal effects on tissue response to plaque. These growths typically shrink or resolve after delivery but should be monitored by your dentist.

## Safety of Dental Treatment During Pregnancy

Pregnant women can safely receive necessary dental care. The safest trimester for routine dental treatment is the second trimester, when major fetal development is complete but the mother is still comfortable. Emergency treatment can be performed during any trimester if necessary.

Local anesthesia is safe during pregnancy, particularly lidocaine with epinephrine in standard doses. Avoid anesthetics with vasoconstrictors when possible, though their limited use is unlikely to cause problems. Avoid nitrous oxide (laughing gas) during pregnancy, as some studies suggest potential risks.

## X-Ray Safety

Dental X-rays are extremely safe during pregnancy. The radiation dose is minimal, and the X-ray beam is focused on the mouth far from the developing baby. Additionally, a lead apron provides extra protection. Don't delay necessary X-rays—they may be essential for diagnosis and treatment.

## Antibiotic Safety

Many antibiotics are safe during pregnancy. Penicillin-based antibiotics are safe and remain first-line choices for dental infections. Amoxicillin is commonly prescribed and safe. Avoid tetracycline antibiotics, which can discolor developing teeth, and fluoroquinolones, which have insufficient safety data.

If you require antibiotics for a dental infection, your dentist will select pregnancy-safe options. Untreated dental infections pose greater risk than appropriate antibiotics, so don't refuse necessary treatment.

## Routine Care During Pregnancy

Continue brushing twice daily and flossing daily throughout pregnancy. Use a soft-bristled brush with gentle technique, as hormonal changes make gums more prone to trauma. If you experience morning sickness, rinse your mouth with water or salt water after vomiting—don't brush immediately, as stomach acid temporarily softens enamel.

Schedule regular dental cleanings during pregnancy, particularly important because hormonal changes increase gum disease risk. Professional cleaning removes tartar and bacteria, reducing gum inflammation. Most dentists recommend cleaning at least once during pregnancy, preferably twice.

## Managing Pregnancy Gingivitis

Excellent oral hygiene is most important for managing pregnancy gingivitis. Brush thoroughly twice daily. Floss daily, despite increased bleeding—flossing removes the plaque irritating gums. Antimicrobial rinses may help reduce inflammation.

Your dentist may recommend more frequent cleanings or additional professional treatments to manage inflammation. Don't be alarmed by increased bleeding—it's common during pregnancy and usually improves with good home care and professional cleaning.

## Dietary Considerations

Pregnancy often increases cravings for sweet or acidic foods. Limit sugary snacks and acidic beverages that promote cavities. Frequent snacking throughout the day increases decay risk more than consumption with meals. Choose nutritious snacks like cheese, nuts, and fruits.

Adequate calcium and vitamin D are important for fetal development and your own bone health. Dairy products, leafy greens, and fortified foods provide these nutrients. Vitamin D supplementation may be recommended by your obstetrician.

## Nausea Management

Many pregnant women experience morning sickness or nausea. If you vomit, don't brush your teeth immediately—stomach acid temporarily softens enamel. Instead, rinse with water or salt water. Wait 30 minutes before brushing, allowing enamel to re-harden.

If nausea makes brushing difficult, use a smaller toothbrush and brush more gently. Some women tolerate electric toothbrushes better. Use fluoride toothpaste despite nausea, as fluoride protection is important during pregnancy.

## Postponing Elective Procedures

Cosmetic procedures should be postponed until after delivery. Teeth whitening and veneers can wait. However, restorative procedures needed to prevent or treat disease should not be delayed. Your dentist will determine what's essential versus elective.

## Postpartum Considerations

After delivery, hormonal changes allow gum tissues to return to normal. Pregnancy gingivitis typically improves within weeks. If you developed pregnancy tumors, they usually resolve without treatment as hormones normalize.

Continue excellent oral hygiene postpartum, particularly important if you're breastfeeding, as maternal health affects milk quality. Schedule a dental visit after delivery to address any problems and ensure your oral health is optimized.

## When to See Your Dentist

Visit your dentist early in pregnancy to establish baseline health and address any problems. Schedule a cleaning during the second trimester. Contact your dentist immediately if you develop severe pain, significant swelling, or other concerning symptoms—these may indicate infections requiring urgent treatment.
