## Introduction

A night guard (occlusal splint) is one of the most effective and affordable treatments for TMD, working in 70-80% of patients within 2-4 weeks. This guide explains the different types of splints, how they work, what to expect, and how to maximize results. A well-fitted splint can be life-changing—less morning soreness, easier opening, reduced pain, and fewer headaches.

## How Splints Actually Work

A custom-fitted night guard works through several mechanisms:

**Reduced parafunctional activity:** The hard acrylic surface makes you AWARE when you're clenching, so you consciously relax. This awareness breaking the clenching cycle is huge.

**Improved bite stability:** Even contact on all teeth reduces stress concentration on individual teeth and the jaw joint.

**Neuromuscular retraining:** Wearing a splint for weeks gradually retrains your jaw muscles and proprioception (sense of position). Your muscles "learn" more efficient, less painful patterns.

**Reduced joint loading:** By stabilizing your bite and reducing clenching force, the joint bears less stress, allowing inflammation to resolve.

**Psychological benefit:** A tangible appliance gives you a sense of control—you're "doing something," which helps.

## Types of Splints: Which One Might Work for You

**Stabilization Splints (Most Common):**

This hard acrylic appliance covers your upper teeth with a smooth, flat occlusal surface. It has simultaneous contact on all teeth in centric relation (your bite's "neutral" position) with freedom to move laterally and forward without splint-induced forces.

**How it works:** Stabilization splints are worn only at night. They immediately reduce morning soreness in 70-80% of people within 1-2 weeks. They're simple, effective, and work for most TMD.

**Best for:** Muscle-based TMD (clenching, grinding), general pain, and most TMD presentations.

**Anterior Repositioning Splints (More Controversial):**

This mandibular splint positions your lower jaw forward (typically 3-7mm) so your teeth contact on the front of the splint, pulling your jaw forward when you close. Learn more about [Tmd Temporomandibular Disorder Overview](/article/tmd-temporomandibular-disorder-overview.html) for additional guidance.

**How it works:** Theoretically, forward positioning reduces stress on posterior joint structures and decompresses anteriorly displaced discs.

**Important caveat:** Evidence is mixed. While some patients improve dramatically, others get worse. Some develop posterior open bite (back teeth don't touch) after long-term wear. Anterior repositioning should be used cautiously and with careful monitoring.

**NTI-tss Devices (Small, Anterior-Only):**

A minimalist device covering only your front teeth with a single contact point on your lower front tooth, keeping all back teeth separated.

**How it works:** Theoretically activates trigeminal nociceptor inhibition (reducing pain signals via anterior tooth contact), but evidence for this mechanism is weak.

**Results:** Comparable to stabilization splints (65-75% improvement) without clear advantage, and risk of anterior open bite development with extended wear.

## Material Matters: Hard Acrylic vs. Soft

**Hard Acrylic (Preferred):**

- Extremely durable (lasts 2-4 years)
- Easy to adjust and refine
- Stable—doesn't deform or absorb water
- Slightly uncomfortable initially but quickly becomes normal
- Cost-effective

Hard acrylic is the standard choice for long-term TMD management.

**Soft Acrylic/Thermoplastic (Limited Use):**

- More comfortable initially
- Easier insertion and removal
- But: deforms over weeks, absorbs water, becomes warped, bacteria colonize readily
- Better for short-term use or patients with severe gag reflex

For long-term TMD treatment, hard acrylic wins.

## Getting a Splint Made: What to Expect

Your dentist takes an impression of your teeth and sends it to a laboratory with specifications: type, tooth coverage, centric relation position, contact pattern, and material preference.

When you get your splint, your dentist performs critical adjustment using articulating paper to ensure simultaneous tooth contact in centric relation, with 1-2mm clearance in lateral and forward movements. Improper adjustment is the #1 cause of splint failure. This adjustment is ESSENTIAL—your dentist should spend 15-20 minutes on it.

## How to Wear Your Splint: The Protocol

**Week 1-2:** Nighttime only, 8+ hours nightly. Remove during eating and morning tooth brushing.

**Week 2-4:** Nighttime wear plus 1-2 hours daytime wear if beneficial.

**Week 4+:** Sustained nighttime wear with optional daytime wear.

## Expected Timeline

**Within 1-2 weeks:** Most people notice morning soreness decreases and opening becomes easier.

**Within 4 weeks:** Significant pain reduction (50%+ in most people), fewer headaches, improved function.

**Beyond 4 weeks:** Continued improvement as muscles adapt. Maximum benefit usually achieved by 8-12 weeks.

## Duration: How Long Do You Need to Wear It?

**Short-term (1-3 months):** Some people achieve lasting improvement with brief wear if they develop better habits.

**Long-term (months to years):** People with persistent clenching/grinding often need extended wear to maintain symptom control.

**Discontinuation trials:** Every 3-6 months, try sleeping without the splint. Progressive symptom return indicates continued need. Many people continue wearing splints indefinitely for prevention.

## Maintenance and Care

**Cleaning:** Daily gentle brushing with a soft toothbrush and mild soap. No hot water.

**Professional cleaning:** Annual professional cleaning removes staining and calculus.

**Replacement:** Hard acrylic splints typically last 2-4 years before requiring replacement.

## Possible Side Effects (Usually Temporary)

**Gag reflex:** Occurs in 10-15%, usually resolves within 1-2 weeks. Reduced palatal extension may help.

**Speech changes:** Minor initially, normalizes within 1-2 weeks.

**Bite sensation:** Slight alteration initially, resolves within 2-4 weeks.

## Maximizing Splint Success

A splint works best combined with:

- Physical therapy: Stretching and relaxation exercises
- Stress management: Meditation, yoga, exercise
- Behavioral modification: Conscious relaxation, habit awareness
- Sleep position: Proper pillow support
- Posture: Forward head posture correction

Splint therapy + behavioral approaches = 85% success.

## Tips for Long-Term Success

Maintaining good results after dental treatment requires consistent care at home and regular professional check-ups. Brushing twice daily with a fluoride toothpaste and flossing at least once a day forms the foundation of good oral hygiene. These simple habits go a long way toward protecting your investment in your dental health and preventing future problems.

Your dentist may recommend additional steps specific to your situation, such as using a special rinse, wearing a nightguard, or adjusting your diet. Following these personalized recommendations can make a significant difference in how well your results hold up over time. Scheduling regular dental visits allows your dentist to catch any developing issues early, when they are easiest and least expensive to address.



*Every patient's situation is unique—always consult your dentist before making treatment decisions.*

## Conclusion

Splint therapy represents an evidence-based, cost-effective conservative treatment option for temporomandibular disorders amenable to appropriate splint type selection and careful fabrication. Stabilization splints remain the gold standard appliance with superior evidence and lower complication risk. Meticulous chairside adjustment establishing simultaneous bilateral contacts and posterior tooth clearance is critical for treatment success. Gradual wear schedule adaptation and combined implementation of physical therapy and behavioral modification enhance therapeutic efficacy.

> **Key Takeaway:** A custom-fitted night guard is the most effective, most affordable, non-invasive TMD treatment available. Stabilization splints work in 70-80% of patients within 2-4 weeks. Success requires meticulous initial adjustment, combined physical therapy and stress management, and consistent wear. Most people find it life-changing.
