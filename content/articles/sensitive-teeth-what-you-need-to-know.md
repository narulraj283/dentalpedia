## Introduction

If your gums are pulling away from your teeth, you've noticed sensitivity, or you can see the yellow/beige roots of your teeth where you used to see only white enamel, you might have gum recession. This is one of the most common causes of tooth sensitivity, and it's also one of the most treatable.

Gum recession isn't just a cosmetic concern—exposed roots are more prone to decay and sensitivity. The good news is that understanding what caused your recession helps you prevent future problems and treat sensitivity effectively.

## Understanding Gum Recession

Gum recession is when the margin of gum tissue pulls away from your tooth, exposing the root. Your gums don't regrow—once they recede, the exposed area stays exposed unless treated professionally. However, recession can be prevented from getting worse, and new recession can be prevented in other areas.

The amount of recession matters. Minor recession (a few millimeters) is usually just uncomfortable and cosmetic. Significant recession (more than a few millimeters) exposes enough root surface that decay risk increases substantially. Very severe recession might even affect the tooth's stability over time.

## Why Your Gums Are Receding

Several factors cause gum recession. Usually, it's a combination rather than a single cause.

**Aggressive Brushing**: This is one of the most common causes, especially if you use a hard-bristled toothbrush or scrub your teeth forcefully. Over time, this physical trauma causes your gums to recede. The solution is simple: switch to a soft-bristled brush and use gentle, small circular motions rather than vigorous back-and-forth scrubbing.

**Gum Disease**: Periodontitis (gum disease) causes loss of bone and soft tissue that support your teeth. Your gums recede as the underlying bone disappears. Controlling gum disease stops recession but doesn't reverse already-lost tissue without professional intervention.

**Natural Aging**: Some recession is part of aging. Your gums naturally become thinner and more fragile as you age, even with perfect home care and no gum disease.

**Tooth Position**: If your teeth are positioned forward in your jaw (protruding), your gums have thinner bone and soft tissue support and are more prone to recession.

**Clenching or Grinding**: This creates excessive force on your teeth and contributes to gum recession over time. A night guard helps protect your teeth and gums from this damage.

**Frenum**: A frenum is a thick band of tissue that connects your gum to your lip. If your frenum is positioned unusually low or is particularly thick, it can pull on your gum and cause recession.

## The Anatomy of Your Tooth: Why Exposed Roots Hurt

To understand tooth sensitivity from recession, you need to know your tooth's anatomy.

Your tooth has three main parts: enamel (the hard white outer layer that covers the crown), dentin (softer material beneath the enamel), and the pulp (soft tissue inside containing nerves and blood vessels).

The root of your tooth is covered by cementum (a softer material similar to bone) rather than hard enamel. Unlike enamel, cementum is softer and doesn't protect the dentin as well. The dentin contains microscopic tubes that run from the outer surface to the nerve. When your root is exposed and these tubes become open, stimulation (cold, heat, pressure, acid, or sweets) can travel through the tubes to your nerve, causing sensitivity.

This is why root sensitivity feels sharper and more intense than enamel-related issues—the tubes provide direct access to the nerve.

## Preventing Further Recession

Once your gums have receded, you can't restore them without professional intervention. However, you can prevent your gums from receding further and prevent new recession in other areas. **Change Your Brushing Technique**: Switch to a soft-bristled toothbrush immediately. Brush with gentle pressure using small circular motions. Electric toothbrushes with pressure sensors help prevent aggressive brushing. Many electric toothbrushes stop automatically if you apply too much pressure.

**Protect Your Gums from Disease**: Brush twice daily and floss daily. These simple habits prevent the gum disease that causes recession. Gum disease is preventable through good home care and regular professional cleanings.

**Address Clenching or Grinding**: If you grind or clench your teeth (especially at night), ask your dentist about a night guard. This protects all your teeth from the excessive force that contributes to recession.

**Manage Frenum Issues**: If your frenum is contributing to recession, your dentist might recommend frenectomy—a simple procedure that removes or reattaches the frenum to a better position.

**Control Gum Disease**: If you have gum disease, getting it under control stops recession immediately. Professional scaling and root planing remove buildup and allow your gums to heal. Combined with excellent home care, this prevents further recession.

## Treating Sensitivity From Exposed Roots

While you can't regrow lost gum tissue without professional help, you can treat the sensitivity that comes with exposed roots.

**Desensitizing Toothpaste**: For exposed roots, desensitizing toothpaste is often the first-line treatment. Use it twice daily, focusing on the sensitive areas. It works by blocking the tubules, preventing stimulation from reaching your nerve. Give it 2-3 weeks to work effectively.

**Fluoride Application**: High-strength professional fluoride is very effective for root sensitivity. Your dentist can apply fluoride gel or varnish to your exposed roots during appointments. Combined with daily desensitizing toothpaste, this provides excellent relief for most people.

**Bonded Resin**: Your dentist can apply tooth-colored resin to cover exposed root surfaces. This is a quick, painless procedure that immediately eliminates sensitivity. The resin typically lasts 5-7 years before needing replacement.

**Gum Graft Surgery**: If recession is severe and cosmetically bothersome, or if other treatments haven't worked, your dentist might recommend graft surgery. This involves taking tissue from elsewhere in your mouth (usually the hard palate) and grafting it onto the area where gums have receded. This covers the exposed root and restores gum tissue. This is more invasive than other options, so it's typically considered when recession is severe or other treatments haven't worked.

## Addressing Cosmetic Concerns

Many people with mild gum recession are mainly bothered by the appearance—they can see the yellow/tan root where white enamel used to be. This is common and very treatable. For more detailed information, see our guide on [gum recession: causes, stages, and treatment options](/article/gum-recession-causes-stages-and-treatment-options.html).

**Cosmetic Bonding**: A tooth-colored bonded resin makes the exposed root match the rest of your tooth. This is quick, painless, and immediately improves appearance while also treating sensitivity. It's the most common cosmetic solution for mild recession.

**Gum Graft**: If recession is extensive, a graft restores the gum tissue and completely eliminates the exposed root appearance. This is more involved but gives the most natural-looking long-term result.

## When to See Your Dentist

If you notice recession, don't wait to see your dentist. Early intervention prevents the problem from getting worse. Your dentist can:

- Assess how much recession you have
- Identify what caused it
- Recommend prevention strategies to prevent further recession
- Recommend treatment for sensitivity
- Address underlying issues like gum disease or clenching

## Conclusion

Gum recession exposes softer root surfaces, causing sensitivity and increasing cavity risk. The most important step is preventing further recession through gentle brushing, treating gum disease, and managing clenching or grinding. Sensitivity from exposed roots is very treatable through desensitizing toothpaste, professional fluoride, or bonded resin. For significant recession, graft surgery restores gum tissue. See your dentist early if you notice recession—prevention of further loss is easier than treating advanced cases.

## Key Takeaway

Exposed tooth roots cause sensitivity because the softer root material contains tubes that lead to your nerve. You can't regrow lost gum tissue at home, but you can prevent further recession, treat sensitivity with desensitizing products and professional fluoride, and permanently cover exposed roots with bonded resin or graft surgery.
