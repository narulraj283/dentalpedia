Immediate Dentures: Teeth on Same Day as Extraction

Understanding Immediate Dentures

Immediate dentures represent a prosthodontic solution that allows patients to leave the dental office with functional teeth on the same day remaining natural teeth are extracted. This advancement eliminates the "toothless gap" period that traditional denture patients experienced. The procedure involves fabricating a complete denture based on pre-extraction dental casts, allowing placement immediately following tooth extraction.

The concept, while not new—practitioners have used immediate dentures since the early 20th century—remains valuable today. Modern immediate denture protocols incorporate contemporary prosthodontic principles, digital imaging, and refined fabrication techniques to optimize outcomes. For many patients facing complete tooth loss, the psychological and practical benefits of immediate teeth restoration prove invaluable.

How Immediate Dentures Differ from Conventional Dentures

Conventional complete dentures require 3-8 weeks after tooth extraction before fabrication can begin, allowing initial tissue healing. The dentist then takes final impressions and measurements, initiates denture fabrication, and delivers the prosthesis several weeks later. This timeline creates a toothless interval lasting 2-3 months.

Immediate dentures, conversely, are fabricated before tooth extraction. The dentist takes impressions with remaining teeth present, records bite relationships with existing dentition, and produces the denture on stone casts before extraction occurs. At the delivery appointment, the remaining teeth are extracted, and the denture is immediately inserted. This approach maintains continuity of function and appearance.

Clinical Advantages

The primary advantage is psychological. Patients avoid the edentulous period, maintaining normal appearance and function. This benefit extends to employment and social situations—patients can return to work the next day without visible changes in appearance or obvious absence of teeth.

Practical advantages include maintained speech and eating ability. While adjustment is still necessary, patients never experience complete functional loss. This eases dietary and nutritional management during the critical healing period.

Immediate dentures can reduce overall treatment time. While many dentists still recommend a conventional denture approach, understanding immediate denture benefits allows patients and practitioners to make informed decisions.

Fabrication Process

The immediate denture fabrication process begins with detailed pre-extraction assessment. The dentist obtains precise impressions with existing teeth present, using impression materials that capture detailed anatomy. Bite registration occurs with maximum intercuspation of natural teeth.

Diagnostic casts are created on which tooth removal is simulated. Using stone models, the dentist marks teeth designated for extraction, then carefully removes them from the master cast to create the edentulous model. The immediate denture is then fabricated on this modified cast.

Accuracy is critical. Any discrepancy in tooth positioning during simulated extraction compromises fit. Experienced prosthodontists have refined techniques using photographic guides and precise marking systems to ensure accuracy within 0.5-1mm of actual tissue contours.

What to Expect at Delivery

Extraction and denture insertion occur sequentially. The dentist typically extracts teeth quickly, minimizing bleeding and maintaining visibility. Immediate socket bleeding is normal and expected—gauze packing may be necessary. The denture is inserted while tissues are still slightly edematous (swollen).

Initial fit is intentionally approximate rather than perfect. The denture will rest on healing tissues that will continue changing for weeks. Immediate dentures intentionally have slightly loose fit compared to conventional dentures because tissues will resorb as healing progresses.

Patients receive detailed post-insertion instructions. Ice application for the first 24-48 hours reduces swelling. The denture should remain in place initially to maintain correct position during critical early healing. Rinsing with warm salt water (1/2 teaspoon salt in 8oz water) four times daily promotes healing.

Pain management is important. Over-the-counter analgesics like ibuprofen or acetaminophen manage discomfort. Prescription analgesics are occasionally necessary for severe pain.

Adjustment and Adaptation Period

The first week is critical. Significant tissue resorption occurs, particularly during days 1-5. A return visit on day 3-5 is essential for tissue assessment and denture adjustment. The dentist may add tissue conditioner—a soft, moldable material—to fill spaces created by tissue resorption.

Subsequent appointments at 1 week, 2 weeks, and 4 weeks allow progressive adjustments. Tissue conditioning, selective pressure areas, and denture border adjustments occur at these visits. This intensive adjustment schedule distinguishes immediate denture care from conventional denture delivery.

The adjustment period typically extends 2-3 months. Initial accelerated healing and resorption gradually stabilize. By 3 months, tissues have undergone most resorption. A definitive reline—replacing the tissue surface of the denture with new acrylic accurately fitting healed tissues—is then performed.

Limitations and Considerations

Immediate dentures require more skilled prosthodontic intervention than conventional dentures. The extraction simulation on dental casts cannot perfectly predict actual tissue topography. Some initial ill fit is inevitable and requires professional correction.

Cost is higher than conventional denture therapy. The additional fabrication steps, simulated extraction technique, and intensive adjustment protocol increase total treatment time and expense.

Patients must commit to frequent adjustment visits. Unlike conventional denture patients who return occasionally for adjustments, immediate denture patients need multiple visits within the critical first 2 months.

Psychological adjustment remains necessary. While appearance and function are maintained, sensation and motor control of dentures take time to develop. Some patients struggle with denture retention and stability initially.

Who Benefits Most From Immediate Dentures

Patients who cannot tolerate edentulous periods benefit most—those in public-facing professions, individuals with significant psychological concerns about tooth loss, or patients with limited social support. Those requiring rapid return to work or normal activities often find immediate dentures preferable.

Additionally, immediate dentures allow patients to practice denture care during the healing period when frequent professional adjustment occurs anyway. Dentists can provide instruction during these visits, building patient confidence and competence.

Post-Delivery Care and Follow-Up

Following denture delivery, meticulous oral hygiene prevents complications. Daily rinsing removes food particles. Denture cleaning with a soft brush and mild soap (not abrasive pastes) maintains denture integrity.

Smoking delays healing significantly and increases peri-implantitis risk in patients later choosing implants. Complete smoking cessation during the healing period improves outcomes dramatically.

Nutritional support during healing is important. Soft foods provide necessary nutrients while minimizing chewing forces on healing tissues. Adequate protein aids tissue repair.

Transitioning to Long-Term Care

After 3-4 months, when healing is complete and tissues are relatively stable, a definitive reline ensures optimal fit. This may occur in the original denture or be deferred until future maintenance.

Regular maintenance appointments every 6-12 months allow professional assessment of tissue changes, denture fit, and necessary adjustments. Many patients eventually transition to implant-supported restorations, which immediate dentures make possible by allowing time for consideration of long-term options.

Making Your Decision

Immediate dentures offer a valuable option for patients facing complete tooth loss who wish to avoid the edentulous period. Discussion with an experienced prosthodontist about your specific needs, expectations, and commitment to intensive early care ensures you make the right choice for your situation.
