Mouth Guard Fitting in Athletes

Orofacial trauma represents one of the leading causes of dental injury in pediatric and adolescent populations, with approximately 20-30% of children experiencing traumatic dental injuries during childhood years. Contact and collision sports including hockey, American football, rugby, boxing, basketball, and soccer account for substantial portions of sports-related oral injuries. Yet many young athletes participate in high-risk sports without proper mouthguard protection despite clear evidence demonstrating 60% or greater reduction in dental and facial injury risk with properly fitted and consistently worn protection. Custom-fitted mouthguards provide substantially superior protection compared to stock appliances through optimized material thickness, contoured fit ensuring shock distribution, and retention characteristics preventing dislodgement during impact.

Epidemiology and Injury Prevention Rationale

Sports-related orofacial trauma encompasses diverse injury patterns affecting teeth, supporting structures, lips, tongue, and mandible. Traumatic tooth fracture represents the most common dental injury, with luxation, intrusion, and avulsion occurring at lower but still significant frequencies. Soft tissue injuries including laceration, contusion, and avulsion of lips and tongue are extremely common in contact sports. Mandibular fractures, though less frequent than dental injuries, create serious functional, aesthetic, and health complications requiring complex management.

The mouthguard's primary function involves distributing impact forces across wider surface areas and dissipating energy through material deformation, reducing force concentration at specific dental or skeletal structures. Laboratory testing and computational modeling demonstrate that properly constructed and fitted mouthguards reduce impact force transmission to dental structures by 50-60% compared to no protection. Additional biomechanical effects including mandibular stabilization may contribute to reduced concussion risk through prevention of excessive head acceleration following mandibular impact.

Mouthguard Types and Comparative Effectiveness

Stock mouthguards purchased without customization offer minimal protection despite low cost. Minimal contact with dental surfaces results in incomplete coverage, poor force distribution, and inadequate thickness over critical areas. Inconsistent fit creates retention problems causing dislodgement during athletic activity when protection is needed most. Poor occlusal fit interferes with breathing and communication, reducing athlete compliance. Clinical evidence demonstrates stock mouthguards provide only 10-20% injury prevention benefit compared to no protection.

Boil-and-bite thermoplastic mouthguards, available over-the-counter or through athletic retailers, offer moderate improvement over stock appliances through mold-fitting allowing basic individual adaptation. However, most patients achieve suboptimal fit using the heating and self-molding process, with common errors including insufficient material removal creating interference with occlusion, improper palatal contour, and inadequate retention features. Studies comparing clinical outcomes between boil-and-bite appliances and custom-made guards demonstrate superior protection with professionally made appliances, though boil-and-bite options may be acceptable when custom fabrication is inaccessible.

Laboratory-constructed custom mouthguards fabricated from dental models provide optimal protection through precision fit, material thickness optimization, and professional construction techniques. Constructed from acrylic resin or thermoplastic materials using pressure molding or vacuum adaptation, custom guards provide complete dental coverage, optimal force distribution, excellent retention characteristics, and occlusal compatibility. The customization process allows accommodation of individual anatomical variation, erupting teeth status in growing athletes, and orthodontic appliances.

Custom Mouthguard Fabrication Process

Fabrication begins with accurate impression or digital scan capturing all upper teeth, palatal morphology, and adjacent soft tissues. Impressions or scans must achieve sufficient detail to allow clear visualization of all tooth surfaces and interdental areas. Some practitioners capture impressions with the athlete's habitual occlusion to ensure optimal mouthguard fit against individual tooth positions; others prefer tooth alignment correction through laboratory procedures improving mouthguard contour.

Model construction from impressions must demonstrate excellent dimensional accuracy with sharp detail preservation to enable precise mouthguard adaptation. Some laboratories employ digital modeling with three-dimensional scanning and computer-aided design enabling custom thickness distribution—areas receiving direct impact forces incorporate greater thickness while areas away from high-impact zones employ thinner material reducing bulk and improving comfort and speaking ability.

Material selection typically involves acrylic resin or thermoplastic copolymer. Acrylic resin provides excellent force attenuation, outstanding durability, superior color customization enabling team colors or patterns, and easy laboratory adjustments and repairs. Thermoplastic materials offer improved comfort, easier clinical adjustment, and flexibility characteristics potentially improving retention; however, they demonstrate greater material degradation over extended time. Athletic mouthguards typically last 1-2 years before replacement becomes necessary due to material deterioration, physical damage, or accommodation changes from tooth eruption and growth.

Thickness considerations balance protection optimization against comfort and functional acceptability. Optimal thickness over tooth incisal edges approximates 4-5mm, with slightly thinner material over facial surfaces (2-3mm) reducing bulk while maintaining force attenuation. The palatal surface should achieve 3-4mm thickness creating effective shock absorption without excessive palatal impingement. Retention features including palatal and buccal surface contours, posterior extension to the tuberosity or retromolar area, and interproximal space adaptation all contribute to mouthguard retention during impact.

Clinical Fitting and Retention Assessment

Laboratory fabrication creates the foundation, but clinical fitting verification ensures optimal protection and functionality. Proper fit assessment evaluates complete dental coverage with no exposed tooth structure vulnerable to direct impact trauma. Retention evaluation determines whether the mouthguard maintains position on the dental arch during functional movement including swallowing, speaking, and simulated impact. Severely deficient retention allowing dislodgement during athletic activity defeats protective purpose; however, extremely tight retention creating discomfort and difficulty insertion/removal discourages usage.

Optimal retention allows 1-2mm movement from fully seated position but maintains contact throughout all tooth surfaces. Labial surface contours should achieve intimate contact with tooth surfaces without creating pressure areas or blanching of attached gingiva indicating excessive force. Palatal surface adaptation must achieve good contact while avoiding significant palatal impingement causing gagging or discomfort affecting compliance. Interproximal areas should demonstrate good material adaptation preventing biofilm trapping or soft tissue impingement.

Occlusal contact assessment ensures the mouthguard does not interfere with normal bite closure. Severely interfering mouthguards force the athlete into anterior-positioned occlusion, creating temporomandibular joint stress, jaw muscle fatigue, and functional problems. Modern custom fabrication often includes occlusal relief creating slight disocclusion (separation) when mouthguard is fully seated, preventing bite interference while protecting teeth through covering dental surfaces.

Adjustments following delivery address fit problems. Diamond burs and acrylic instruments remove material from areas causing excessive pressure or impingement. Addition of retentive features through acrylic buildup improves inadequate retention. Proper trimming removes excess material that would interfere with breathing, speaking, or swallowing. Athletes should be able to speak clearly, breathe easily, and swallow saliva without difficulty; if any of these functions are substantially compromised, further adjustments become necessary.

Athlete Compliance and Long-Term Usage

Mouthguard protection efficacy depends critically on consistent usage during athletic activity. Studies examining compliance demonstrate that 25-35% of athletes who own mouthguards do not use them consistently. Barriers to compliance include discomfort, impaired breathing or speaking, difficulty insertion/removal, and behavioral factors including forgetting or peer pressure. Custom-fitted appliances demonstrating superior comfort and functional acceptability demonstrate higher compliance rates compared to stock or boil-and-bite options, validating the investment in professional fabrication.

Athlete education emphasizing injury consequences—dental trauma causing tooth loss, root damage, extraction, or complex restorative treatment requiring years to complete—motivates compliance. Many young athletes fail to appreciate the permanent consequences of preventable dental trauma until experiencing injury themselves. Parental involvement in compliance oversight, especially for young athletes, supports consistent usage. Some athletic programs include mouthguard-wearing requirements in team rules and pre-game equipment checks.

Care instructions extending mouthguard longevity and hygiene include rinsing with cool water after use, storing in ventilated containers preventing moisture accumulation and mold growth, and avoiding heat exposure that warps thermoplastic materials. Athletes should inspect mouthguards regularly for damage including cracks, material separation, or degradation. Minor defects may be repaired; extensive damage necessitates replacement. Mouthguards typically require replacement annually or after significant trauma to ensure ongoing protective efficacy.

Special Circumstances and Considerations

Athletes with fixed orthodontic appliances require specially constructed mouthguards incorporating relief over bracket and wire surfaces. Standard appliance areas require thicker material providing protection for bracket wings and wires while maintaining overall comfort. Removable appliance wearers should insert mouthguards after removal to prevent appliance damage or loss. Upon appliance removal and tooth alignment completion, new mouthguards become necessary accommodating changed tooth positions.

Athletes with missing teeth or significant restorations require custom fabrication considering existing dental work. Mouthguards must provide coverage and retention around implants, crown-bridge prosthetics, or composite restorations without creating retention problems or interfering with occlusion.

Growing athletes undergoing active tooth eruption require annual mouthguard reassessment and replacement to accommodate erupting permanent teeth. Mouthguards fabricated for partially erupted dentitions may not provide optimal coverage as eruption completes. Annual updating ensures consistent protection throughout growth periods.

Female athletes require attention to specific protective concerns; while dental trauma rates are generally lower in female-participation sports, those engaging in contact sports receive equivalent benefit from mouthguard protection as male athletes. Mouthguard customization can accommodate specific aesthetic preferences including color selection and decoration, improving acceptance in populations where athletic culture initially framed mouthguards as exclusively masculine.
