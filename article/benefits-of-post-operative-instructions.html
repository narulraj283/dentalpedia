Recovery Instructions After Oral Surgery

Following your dentist's post-operative instructions is as important as the surgery itself. What you do (or don't do) in the first few days dramatically affects healing speed, pain levels, and whether complications develop. Your surgical team will give you detailed instructions, but here are the general guidelines for different types of procedures.

Right After Tooth Extraction

First 30-45 minutes: Bite down on gauze with firm (but not crushing) pressure. Keep it dry—don't let saliva soak it too much. If bleeding continues, you might need to change the gauze once or twice. This period is critical for clot formation. The blood clot is your body's natural protection for the wound, so keeping it undisturbed is essential. First 24 hours:
  • Rest completely. No work, no exercise, no activities. Your heart rate elevation increases bleeding. Rest with your head elevated on 2-3 pillows, not flat. Elevation matters because it reduces blood pressure in the head, which reduces bleeding.
  • Soft foods and liquids only. Soups, smoothies, yogurt, ice cream. Nothing hot (heat increases bleeding and inflammation). No straws (the suction can dislodge the blood clot). Don't spit—just let saliva drool into a tissue. Suction is the enemy of newly formed clots.
  • Ice first 24 hours: 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off. This reduces swelling and pain by constricting blood vessels. The first 24 hours is when you can make the biggest difference in swelling, so don't skip this.
  • No smoking or alcohol for at least 72 hours, ideally a week. Both increase bleeding and prevent healing. Smoking reduces oxygen to the healing tissue. Alcohol thins blood and interferes with immune response.
Days 2-5:
  • Ice becomes less helpful after 24 hours because swelling peaks at 48-72 hours naturally. Switch to warmth (warm compress) to improve swelling and circulation. Heat brings blood flow to promote healing.
  • Gentle salt water rinses (1/2 teaspoon salt in 8oz warm water) start 24 hours post-extraction. Rinse gently after meals. This cleans the area without disturbing the clot. Start day 2, not day 1.
  • Diet progresses to soft foods as comfort allows. No hard, sticky, or crunchy foods. By day 3-4, many people can eat soft solids.
  • Brushing: Avoid the extraction site completely, but gently brush adjacent teeth. Oral hygiene matters but the extraction site needs peace.
Healing timeline: The socket epithelializes (covers over) by day 5, but complete healing takes 3-6 months. Don't expect it to be healed just because you're comfortable—internal healing is still ongoing for months.

After Implant Placement

First 2 weeks: This is completely different from extraction recovery. Implants need peace and quiet to integrate with bone.
  • NO pressure on the implant site. Don't chew there, don't press it with your tongue, don't mess with it. Your implant is integrating with bone through a process called osseointegration. Pressure interferes with this crucial process.
  • Ice for 24 hours, then gentle heat.
  • Soft diet for 6-8 weeks (yes, weeks). The implant needs the healing space protected during osseointegration.
  • Head elevation first week.
  • Chlorhexidine rinse (0.12%) twice daily for 1-2 weeks. Gentle rinsing for 1 minute each time—don't swish aggressively.
  • Sutures come out in 7-10 days.
  • Full diet resumption around 8-12 weeks when osseointegration is more advanced.
The key difference: With extraction, you're healing a socket. With implants, you're trying to get bone to fuse to a titanium screw. That requires undisturbed healing. Premature chewing can dislodge the implant. Biting pressure before integration completes can cause implant failure.

After Gum Surgery

First 1-2 weeks: Don't brush or floss the surgical site. Your gums are repositioned and need time to reattach. This is different from extractions where you want some disturbance to prevent complications.
  • Chlorhexidine rinse (0.12%) twice daily for 1-2 weeks. This is really important to prevent infection in a surgical wound.
  • Soft diet for 1-2 weeks.
  • Swelling peaks at 48-72 hours, which is normal.
  • Heat application (warm compress) helps swelling after the first 24 hours.
  • Gentle resumption of brushing at 10-14 days when tissues are stronger.
Gum surgery wounds are different from tooth extraction wounds because the gum tissue is repositioned, not just healing in place. Extra care prevents complications.

Wisdom Teeth Recovery (Special Considerations)

Wisdom teeth extraction is more aggressive than single-tooth extraction. Expect:

  • Liquid diet first 24 hours, soft foods days 2-3. Wisdom teeth extraction often involves bone removal and more trauma.
  • Aggressive swelling management: Ice first 24 hours, then heat, compression bandages 3-4 hours post-op, head elevation for 3 nights. Multiple wisdom teeth extractions cause more swelling than single extractions.
  • Trismus (limited jaw opening): Ice first 24 hours, gentle stretching exercises after, heat after 24 hours. Usually resolves in 5-7 days. This is muscle tightness in response to surgical trauma. Gentle movement (not forced) helps resolution.
  • Dry socket: Rare (5-10%) but painful. Avoid smoking (increases risk 3-4 times), follow post-op instructions carefully, and contact your dentist immediately if pain gets worse on days 3-5 instead of better. Dry socket happens when the clot dislodges. Once it happens, your dentist can treat it, but prevention is much easier than treatment.

Warning Signs That Need Immediate Attention

Contact your dentist or seek emergency care if you have:

  • Uncontrolled bleeding beyond 2 hours (indicates something wrong)
  • Fever above 101°F (suggests infection)
  • Progressive swelling that gets worse instead of better after day 2-3 (suggests infection or hematoma)
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing (go to ER—potential airway issue)
  • Numbness extending beyond the first week (might indicate nerve injury)
  • Pus or discharge from the surgical site (suggests infection)

Don't tough it out if you have these symptoms. Call your dentist immediately.

Your Role in Recovery

You're not passive—you actively help healing by:

  • Following gauze pressure instructions exactly
  • Taking ibuprofen-acetaminophen on schedule (even when pain is mild) to control inflammation
  • Applying ice/heat as directed
  • Keeping your head elevated while sleeping
  • Avoiding strenuous activity that elevates blood pressure
  • Following dietary restrictions
  • Using chlorhexidine rinse if prescribed (antimicrobial protection)
  • Attending your follow-up appointment for suture removal

Each of these actions directly affects your healing trajectory. Skip them and you're significantly extending your recovery.

The Follow-Up Visit

Your dentist will call 24 hours after surgery to check on swelling and pain. You'll come back in 7-10 days to have sutures removed and verify healing is progressing normally. These visits catch complications early when they're easy to treat. If something looks wrong, they can address it immediately.

At this visit, your dentist checks:

  • Blood clot status (is it stable or showing signs of dry socket?)
  • Infection signs (fever, pus, excess swelling)
  • Healing tissue appearance
  • Suture removal (usually painless)
  • Pain control adequacy

This is why follow-up appointments matter—they're not optional.

Special Situations

If you're diabetic: Healing might be slower. Follow instructions even more carefully. High blood sugar impairs healing, so monitor glucose levels. If you're on blood thinners: Bleeding might be heavier initially. Pressure with gauze might need to last longer. Your dentist will adjust instructions for your specific medications. If you're anxious about pain: Tell your dentist. They can adjust pain medication prescriptions or suggest anxiety management. Anxiety actually makes pain perception worse.

Bottom Line

Post-operative instructions aren't suggestions—they're based on how healing works biologically. Following them prevents complications, reduces pain, speeds healing, and gets you back to normal faster. The 10-14 days after surgery are critical. Take them seriously, follow instructions exactly, and contact your dentist if something seems wrong.
