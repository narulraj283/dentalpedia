Surgical Anatomy and Preoperative Assessment

Unilateral cleft lip (with or without cleft palate) creates specific anatomic derangements requiring precise surgical reconstruction. The orbicularis oris muscle—which normally maintains oral competence and forms the functional oral sphincter—is disrupted, with medial and lateral segments separated by the cleft. The nasal deformity includes: alar base widening and lateral deviation away from the cleft (exacerbated by unopposed action of dilator naris muscle); nasal septum deviation toward the non-cleft side; and inferior turbinate enlargement on the cleft side. The lip length discrepancy (cleft side appearing shorter) results from interruption of muscle and soft tissue continuity. The vermilion border (mucocutaneous junction defining lip outline) shows discontinuity, creating cosmetically visible asymmetry. Preoperative assessment includes: comprehensive clinical examination documenting anatomic measurements (lip height, alar base width, septal deviation, vermilion asymmetry) via standardized photography; airway evaluation for obstructive sleep apnea (documented in 20-35% of cleft patients); hemoglobin determination (operative candidates require hemoglobin >10 g/dL); and general medical clearance. Three-dimensional imaging (cone-beam CT) is increasingly utilized to quantify skeletal involvement, particularly in bilateral cases or when palatal extension complicates the deformity. Baseline photography with standardized lighting and patient positioning enables objective comparison with postoperative results and documentation of surgical outcomes.

Timing of Primary Cleft Lip Repair

The optimal timing for primary unilateral cleft lip repair has evolved from historical protocols advocating delayed repair (age 3-5 years) toward contemporary earlier intervention. The "rule of 10s" (10 weeks of age, 10 pounds of weight, 10 grams hemoglobin) has been largely superseded by earlier repair protocols based on evidence demonstrating improved outcomes with earlier intervention. Current recommendations suggest primary repair ideally at 8-12 weeks of age in healthy infants meeting medical clearance criteria. Early repair (before 3 months) offers advantages including: improved muscle healing with minimal fibrosis and scarring; better nasal molding while cartilage remains relatively plastic; optimized parental-infant bonding; and potential speech development benefits (though primary lip repair alone does not substantially impact speech given that palatal repair is the critical determinant). Delaying repair beyond 6 months increases scar formation tendency and reduces esthetic outcomes. Preterm infants should have repair timing based on corrected age (chronologic age minus weeks of prematurity) with weight thresholds met; typically this results in repair timing of 12-16 weeks chronologic age for previously premature infants. Small-for-gestational-age infants similarly require achievement of weight threshold (minimum 10 pounds) before proceeding, even if chronologic age would otherwise be appropriate. Severe medical comorbidities (uncontrolled cardiac disease, significant pulmonary compromise, severe gastroesophageal reflux) may necessitate delayed repair, though these are uncommon in isolated cleft lip without additional syndromic features.

Surgical Approaches and Primary Lip Repair Techniques

Multiple surgical approaches for unilateral cleft lip repair have achieved widespread acceptance, differing primarily in incision placement and exact muscle dissection/repair sequence. The Millard rotation-advancement technique remains widely employed, utilizing: closure incision placed within natural facial creases (nasolabial fold region and upper lip vermilion border) to maximize scar camouflage; medial flap advancement and rotation to lengthen the cleft side of lip; and lateral flap positioning to achieve symmetry. Alternative approaches include: Mohler's technique (modified rotation-advancement with emphasis on precise anatomic reconstruction); straight-line repair techniques; and variations emphasizing different anatomic priorities. Common surgical principles across all accepted approaches include: (1) creation of precise mucosal flaps with exact alignment of vermilion borders; (2) meticulous muscle (orbicularis oris) dissection and repair to restore continuity and function; (3) reconstruction of oral commissure angle; (4) nasal alar base repositioning to correct cleft-side deviation and widening; (5) nasal floor reconstruction when cleft extends into nasal cavity; and (6) tension-free closure of all layers (mucosa, muscle, skin) to optimize healing and minimize scar hypertrophy. Tissue handling emphasizing minimal trauma, preservation of vascular supply, and judicious use of cautery improves healing and esthetic outcomes. Suture selection typically employs absorbable materials (4.0 or 5.0 chromic catgut or polyglactin for mucosa and muscle, with 6.0 fast-absorbing gut for mucocutaneous junction) to avoid suture removal trauma in young infants; skin closure may utilize absorbable sutures (5.0 or 6.0 fast-absorbing gut) or fine monofilament sutures (6.0 nylon) for optimal cosmesis.

Bilateral Cleft Lip Repair: Sequencing and Special Considerations

Bilateral complete cleft lip presents greater surgical complexity due to the presence of a free-floating premaxilla (vomer-dependent but lacking muscular/osseous lateral support), bilateral muscle disruption, and severe nasal deformity. Bilateral repair requires surgeon decision regarding staging versus simultaneous repair. Simultaneous bilateral repair (both sides in single operation) offers advantage of reduced total operative burden and anesthetic exposures; however, it increases operative time (typically 2.5-3.5 hours) and may increase blood loss. Staged repair (typically right side at initial operation, left side at interval of 3-6 weeks) allows more conservative dissection per operation, reduced blood loss per procedure, and potentially improved primary healing with reduced tension. Whichever sequencing is selected, fundamental surgical principles remain: symmetric orbicularis oris muscle reconstruction, restoration of bilateral nasal alar symmetry, and correction of premaxillary projection (which commonly protrudes excessively in bilateral cases). The bilateral deformity frequently necessitates preliminary maxillary molding (using orthodontic plates with specific pressure patterns) during the first 4-6 weeks of life to move the premaxilla backward and reduce the distance between cleft margins, thereby reducing operative difficulty. Contemporary evidence supports that preliminary molding may reduce operative time by 20-30 minutes and improve initial alignment, though outcomes appear equivalent with excellent surgical technique regardless of preliminary molding.

Postoperative Management and Early Healing Protocols

Postoperative management focuses on protecting the surgical repair from mechanical trauma while supporting normal infant development and feeding. Immediate postoperative care includes: close monitoring of airway (particularly important given cleft-associated anatomic airway challenges); careful extubation technique (extubation with patient awake is standard to maintain airway protection); appropriate positioning to prevent aspiration; and pain management via multimodal analgesia (local anesthetic infiltration, acetaminophen 15 mg/kg every 4-6 hours, potentially opioids 0.05-0.1 mg/kg every 4-6 hours if needed). Arm restraints (soft splints preventing hand-to-mouth contact) are maintained for minimum 5-7 days post-operatively to reduce incision trauma from infant manipulation. Feeding protocols require careful planning: infant should remain NPO (nothing by mouth) 4-6 hours preoperatively, with clear liquids permitted up to 2 hours before scheduled surgery. Postoperatively, feeding recommences within 4-6 hours if infant is alert and able to maintain airway; soft feeding techniques (breast milk from bottle with wide nipple opening, or direct breastfeeding if maternal comfort achieved) minimize suture strain. Feeding should be performed slowly with frequent pauses to prevent aspiration or excessive intraoral pressure. Oral hygiene care involves gentle cleansing with water-dampened gauze after feeding to remove accumulated debris without disrupting sutures; antiseptic rinses are contraindicated early postoperatively. Suture removal timing varies: absorbable sutures dissolve spontaneously over 2-3 weeks; non-absorbable sutures typically remain 5-7 days and require careful removal using appropriate instruments to minimize trauma. Wound assessment should occur daily for first 2 weeks monitoring for signs of infection (excessive erythema, drainage, warmth); infection rates with appropriate technique remain low (2-5%).

Revision Cleft Lip Surgery: Timing and Indications

Revision procedures address suboptimal primary repair results or emerging secondary deformities requiring aesthetic refinement. Primary revision timing typically occurs at ages 4-6 years (kindergarten age) to allow adequate growth, scar maturation, and tissue healing from primary repair while still permitting correction before school entry. Delaying revision beyond age 7-8 increases esthetic impact during socially sensitive school years; however, overly early revision (before age 3-4) may disrupt scar maturation and necessitate additional procedures. Common indications for revision include: persistent vermilion asymmetry or border discontinuity; residual lip length discrepancy (>1-2 mm); inadequate oral commissure symmetry; persistent nasal asymmetry; hypertrophic or depressed scarring; and muscle dysfunction evident from asymmetric smile or oral incompetence. Some revision procedures can be performed under local anesthesia in cooperative older children (age 5+), while others require general anesthesia. Scar revision techniques include: Z-plasty procedures (redirecting scar into more favorable direction and potentially improving visibility); local tissue rearrangement (moving scar into more concealed regions such as natural facial creases); dermabrasion or laser resurfacing (blending scar into surrounding skin); and full-thickness skin graft replacement (though this carries additional morbidity and is rarely necessary for cleft lip scars). Hypertrophic scars frequently improve with conservative management including silicone ointment application, massage, and sun protection; steroid injection into particularly thick areas may reduce hypertrophy (0.5-1 mL of triamcinolone acetonide 10 mg/mL infiltrated directly into scar tissue, with repeat injections at 3-4 week intervals as needed up to 3-4 treatments).

Nasal Esthetics and Rhinoplasty Considerations

Nasal deformity associated with unilateral cleft lip includes: alar base widening (typically 2-5 mm on cleft side), lateral alar displacement, nasal septum deviation toward non-cleft side, and inferior turbinate enlargement on cleft side. Some nasal correction occurs at time of primary lip repair through alar base repositioning and lateral nasal wall support restoration; however, definitive nasal refinement frequently requires secondary rhinoplasty at ages 14-16 (after cessation of significant nasal growth but before or concurrent with completion of orthodontic treatment). Nasal base narrowing can be achieved through endonasal incisions with careful alar base reposition and suturing; this approach avoids external scarring and achieves 3-4 mm reduction in alar base width. More extensive deformities may require open rhinoplasty (external incision across columella) enabling broader visualization and precise cartilage restructuring. Septal deviation correction (via septoplasty) may improve both nasal function and appearance, particularly important in patients demonstrating obstructive sleep apnea or significant inspiratory obstruction. Turbinate reduction may be performed concurrently if turbinate hypertrophy causes obstruction. Nasal outcomes following appropriate rhinoplasty demonstrate symmetry improvement in 85-90% of cases, though complete symmetry achievement remains difficult given the baseline structural asymmetry of cleft-associated anatomy. Patient satisfaction with nasal outcomes averages 75-80%, reflecting appropriate expectations regarding residual asymmetry given the congenital deformity.

Smile Line Assessment and Muscle Function Evaluation

Postoperative assessment should evaluate smile line (relationship of tooth display to upper lip position during smiling) and commissure symmetry. Normal smile line demonstrates balanced display of approximately 75-100% of upper tooth height; asymmetry may suggest inadequate muscle reconstruction or denervation. Smile assessment involves having patient perform voluntary smile while observing muscle contraction symmetry, tooth display symmetry, and commissure elevation symmetry. Lip competence evaluation assesses ability to maintain lip seal at rest; persistent incompetence may suggest inadequate orbicularis oris reconstruction and may necessitate revision procedure. Oral function assessment includes: feeding efficiency (ability to eat without substantial food spillage or intraoral loss); speech quality (presence of compensatory articulation patterns); and appearance of nasal airflow during speech (should be minimal; excessive flow suggests velopharyngeal insufficiency rather than lip repair insufficiency, indicating palatal pathology). Fine motor control of lip movements (pursing, rounding, lateral movements) should be symmetric; asymmetric movement capability may reflect facial nerve involvement (rare) or inadequate muscle repositioning (more common). These functional assessments should be documented at time of revision planning to justify specific surgical revision approaches and enable objective outcome measurement postoperatively.

Complications and Management Strategies

Infection remains the most common serious complication of cleft lip repair, occurring in 2-5% of cases; risk factors include: poor surgical hemostasis (hematoma providing culture medium), inadequate wound coverage, and excessive tension compromising perfusion. Prevention emphasizes meticulous hemostasis, judicious cautery use, and tension-free closure. If infection develops, aggressive management includes: oral antibiotic initiation (cephalexin 25-50 mg/kg/day divided in 4 doses, or clindamycin 10-15 mg/kg/day if penicillin allergy), incision and drainage if fluctuance develops, and careful repeat wound cleaning. Hematoma (bleeding beneath closed repair) occurs in 1-2% of cases, presenting with bulge and discoloration; small hematomas (≤1 cm) may be observed if coagulation status is normal; larger hematomas require incision and drainage under sterile conditions to prevent secondary infection and scarring. Wound dehiscence (separation of repair) results from excessive tension, infection, or patient trauma; small dehiscences (<5 mm) may heal secondarily, while larger separations require urgent surgical re-repair. Hypertrophic scarring (excessive collagen deposition creating thick, raised scar) occurs in 10-15% of cases and frequently improves spontaneously over 6-12 months with sun protection and massage; steroid injection or laser therapy accelerates improvement if significant. Persistent lip asymmetry post-repair (>1-2 mm lip height difference) warrants careful assessment to determine whether asymmetry is cosmetically significant enough to justify revision; revision timing should be delayed until scar maturity (minimum 6-12 months post-primary repair, ideally 4-6 years). Oral commissure asymmetry (one corner of mouth higher than other) may result from inadequate muscle reconstruction or excessive tension; revision approaches include commissuroplasty with repositioning of oral angle.

Long-Term Outcomes and Esthetic Assessment

Longitudinal outcome studies of primary cleft lip repair demonstrate that 75-85% of patients achieve acceptable symmetric appearance when repaired by experienced surgeons employing evidence-based techniques. Acceptable outcomes are defined as: lip height asymmetry <2 mm, symmetric vermilion border, imperceptible or well-camouflaged scar, and symmetric nasal appearance. Unfavorable outcomes (≥10% of cases) typically result from: inadequate primary repair technique, secondary complications (infection, hematoma), late presentation for repair (beyond 6-12 months), or tissue trauma from patient manipulation post-operatively. Scar appearance improves progressively over 12-18 months as collagen remodels and scar redness/thickness diminishes; final scar assessment should not occur before 18-24 months post-repair. Photographic documentation at multiple intervals (immediately post-operatively, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months) enables objective comparison and outcomes tracking. Objective esthetic scoring systems (such as Asher-McDade scale) enable consistent outcome evaluation across surgeons and institutions. Patient and parent satisfaction with surgical outcomes correlates with realistic preoperative counseling regarding natural residual asymmetry and expected scar appearance; satisfaction rates exceed 80% when appropriate expectations are established. Esthetic outcomes improve further with coordinated palatal repair, orthodontic treatment, and secondary rhinoplasty; comprehensive team management typically achieves superior long-term esthetic results compared to isolated lip repair.

Special Populations and Technical Modifications

Premature infants require specific timing considerations; surgical repair should be based on corrected age (chronologic age minus weeks of prematurity) rather than chronologic age. Infants born before 32 weeks gestation or with birth weight <1500 grams may require delayed repair until corrected age 3-4 months and weight >10 pounds due to increased operative risk. Small-for-gestational-age infants similarly may require weight-based rather than age-based timing. Syndromic cleft patients require comprehensive evaluation for associated anomalies (cardiac, renal, skeletal, cognitive) that may impact surgical timing or technique. Patients with Treacher Collins syndrome or Pierre Robin sequence may have specific airway considerations affecting operative planning. Bilateral cleft palate cases with severe maxillary deficiency may benefit from preliminary osseodistractive therapy before primary repair, allowing reduction of cleft gap and potentially improving repair outcomes. Revision cases (patients with prior suboptimal repair, often performed in developing countries or rural areas) require complete anatomic reassessment and frequently necessitate more extensive dissection and tissue mobilization to correct prior scarring. These revision cases often require staged procedures rather than definitive single-stage correction. Ethnic variations in tissue characteristics and healing patterns should inform surgical approach; some evidence suggests varying propensity for hypertrophic scar formation among populations, potentially justifying modified suture management or scar-preventive protocols in high-risk individuals. Cultural and religious considerations should be respectfully incorporated into preoperative planning and family communication regarding timing and treatment options.
