Lingual Nerve Anatomy and Course

The lingual nerve represents a branch of the posterior division of the mandibular division (CN V3) of the trigeminal nerve. This sensory nerve carries sensation from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, the ventral surface of the tongue, the floor of mouth, and the lingual gingiva and mucosa of the mandible. The anatomical course of the lingual nerve makes it particularly vulnerable to iatrogenic injury during oral and maxillofacial surgical procedures, particularly third molar extraction.

The lingual nerve originates within the infratemporal fossa, arising from the mandibular division of the trigeminal nerve. It travels medially within the infratemporal region before exiting the skull through the lingual foramen (a small opening on the medial aspect of the mandibular ramus). After exiting the mandible, the lingual nerve courses medially, downward, and forward into the oral cavity.

Within the oral cavity, the lingual nerve runs in close proximity to the lingual alveolar crest of the mandibular posterior teeth. This anatomical relationship varies between individuals—in some patients the nerve runs relatively superficially along the crest, while in others it courses in a deeper lingual position. This anatomical variability accounts for differential injury risk during procedures affecting the mandibular posterior region.

The lingual nerve crosses the mylohyoid line of the mandible and runs deep to the mylohyoid muscle as it courses anteriorly to reach the anterior two-thirds of the tongue. It provides motor innervation to no muscles (unlike the hypoglossal nerve), and its function is purely sensory. This purely sensory nature means that lingual nerve injury produces isolated sensory disturbance without motor deficit.

Incidence and Etiology of Lingual Nerve Injury

Lingual nerve injury ranks among the most common complications following mandibular third molar extraction, with incidence estimates ranging from 0.4% to 2.0% of extractions. The wide incidence range reflects differences in surgical technique, case complexity, surgeon experience level, and definition of injury (transient versus permanent). Temporary paresthesia (lasting <6 months) occurs more frequently (1.2-2.0%) than permanent sensory disturbance (0.4-0.6%).

The mechanism of lingual nerve injury during third molar surgery typically involves direct trauma from surgical instrumentation, stretching during flap elevation, or compression from retraction forces applied during the procedure. Removal of impacted mandibular third molars presents substantially higher injury risk compared to erupted teeth, particularly in cases where the tooth is deeply impacted in bone and requires significant surgical trauma.

Risk factors for lingual nerve injury include: extreme impaction depth with bone removal requirements exceeding 5-6mm, inferiorly angulated third molars (requiring lingual bone removal), significant surgical trauma and operating time exceeding 20-30 minutes, inadequate surgical experience, inexperienced surgical assistant, and patient age >30 years (older patients demonstrate slower nerve recovery).

Other procedures capable of causing lingual nerve injury include: mandibular osteotomy for orthognathic surgery, manipulation during alveolar bone augmentation procedures, floor of mouth surgery, and rarely during endodontic procedures if instruments perforate the lingual cortex and impinge on the nerve. Clinicians performing any procedure in the mandibular posterior lingual region must maintain awareness of lingual nerve vulnerability.

Assessment and Sensory Testing Methods

Clinical assessment of potential lingual nerve injury begins with evaluation of sensory function in the territory supplied by the nerve—the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, ventral tongue surface, and lingual floor of mouth. The most commonly employed sensory testing methods include: two-point discrimination testing, light touch sensation, and thermal sensation testing.

Two-point discrimination (2PD) testing involves use of a specialized discriminator (or modified compass-like instrument) to determine the minimum distance at which a patient can perceive two distinct tactile points. Normal lingual sensation demonstrates 2PD values of approximately 2-4mm in the midline and 4-6mm lateral to midline. Elevated 2PD values indicate sensory impairment. Testing should be performed bilaterally and compared to the unaffected side.

Light touch sensation can be assessed with cotton swabs or specialized sensory testing filaments (Semmes-Weinstein monofilaments). The examiner gently touches the lingual surface with the filament or swab and asks the patient to report sensation. Comparison to the contralateral (unaffected) side provides objective baseline. Light touch testing is less specific than 2PD testing but provides rapid clinical assessment.

Thermal sensation testing using hot and cold stimuli—warm water on one cotton swab and ice water on another—provides additional assessment of sensory function. Patients demonstrating reduced thermal discrimination or inability to distinguish hot from cold in the lingual region suggest C-fiber involvement. This testing adds minimal burden while providing complementary information.

Quantitative sensory testing (QST) equipment employing standardized thermal or mechanical stimulation allows more objective measurement of sensory thresholds. While QST provides superior reproducibility compared to clinical testing, cost and time requirements limit its clinical application. QST is primarily employed in research settings or for medico-legal documentation of permanent sensory loss.

Documentation of baseline sensory function immediately after surgery, at 6-week follow-up, 3-month follow-up, 6-month follow-up, and annually thereafter provides objective evidence of recovery progression and ultimate outcome. This documentation is critically important for medicolegal documentation and assessing prognosis.

Injury Mechanisms and Pathophysiology

Lingual nerve injury during third molar extraction occurs through several distinct mechanisms. Direct trauma from bone-removing burs or surgical instruments represents one mechanism—inattention to anatomical landmarks or aggressive bone removal in the lingual region can lacerate or contuse the nerve. Stretch injury occurs when flap retraction or instrumentation pulls the nerve beyond its elastic limit, damaging axons without complete transection.

Compression injury results from retraction forces or edema that compresses the nerve against surrounding bone or soft tissue structures. This mechanism typically produces a more favorable prognosis than laceration since intact nerve sheaths contain swelling and allow gradual recovery. Thermal injury from high-speed instrumentation, though less common than mechanical injury, can cause substantial axonal damage.

Ligation or entrapment occurs when the nerve becomes caught within surgical sutures or trapped within healing tissue. These injuries require identification and release to allow normal sensory recovery. Some authorities advocate careful nerve identification and gentle mobilization during closure to prevent inadvertent ligation.

The extent of pathophysiological injury progresses through recognized stages. Initial injury produces inflammation and edema within nerve sheaths, often resulting in conduction block that causes sensory disturbance despite anatomical continuity. Wallerian degeneration occurs over subsequent weeks as damaged axons degenerate distally from the injury site. Reinnervation—restoration of sensory function through axonal regeneration—may proceed over months to years.

Clinical Presentation and Natural History

Patients with acute lingual nerve injury present with paresthesia, dysesthesia (abnormal sensation), or complete anesthesia of the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, ventral tongue surface, and lingual floor of mouth. The distribution typically matches the nerve's sensory field quite precisely, though some variation exists due to individual anatomical variation in nerve territory.

The onset of sensory disturbance is typically immediate if the injury mechanism involves direct trauma or ligation. Disturbance may develop over hours if the injury mechanism is crush or compression-related, as developing edema accumulates. Patients often report tongue numbness, difficulty with fine motor tasks involving the tongue (whistling, precise articulation of certain phonemes), and occasionally burning or tingling sensation (dysesthesia).

Natural recovery from lingual nerve injury shows predictable patterns. Approximately 40-50% of patients with temporary sensory disturbance demonstrate substantial improvement within 3 months, 70-80% by 6 months, and 85-90% by 12 months. Recovery beyond 12 months occurs in approximately 10-15% of patients, with ultimate recovery rate approaching 95% for injuries that eventually resolve.

Permanent sensory loss (persisting beyond 12 months) occurs in approximately 5-10% of injured patients, depending on injury severity. These patients typically adapt well to permanent paresthesia over time, though some experience chronic neuropathic pain. The clinical reality that most lingual nerve injuries recover spontaneously over 6-12 months has important implications for management decisions.

Management of Acute Lingual Nerve Injury

Immediate management following suspected lingual nerve injury begins with documentation of the event. If significant trauma occurred or the nerve was visibly damaged intraoperatively, immediate consideration of microsurgical repair should occur. Complete nerve lacerations identified intraoperatively warrant primary repair, ideally performed by an oral and maxillofacial surgeon experienced in microsurgery or with referral to a microvascular surgery specialist.

Primary repair involves meticulous surgical exploration under microscopic magnification, identification of nerve stumps, careful removal of devitalized tissue (only minimal trimming should occur), and precise realignment of proximal and distal segments. Repair may employ direct coaptation with minimal suturing or use of microsurgical nerve guides depending on the gap size. Tension-free repair is paramount—excessive tension at the repair site substantially worsens prognosis.

For crush injuries or stretch injuries where the nerve is anatomically continuous, immediate surgical exploration is not indicated. Conservative management with careful observation for sensory recovery is appropriate. Patients should be counseled that spontaneous recovery occurs in the majority of cases, with recovery timeline typically ranging 3-12 months. Follow-up sensory testing at 6-week, 3-month, and 6-month intervals documents recovery progression.

Patient counseling is critically important. Patients should be informed that most sensory disturbances resolve spontaneously, but recovery requires 6-12 months. They should understand adaptation strategies such as careful eating to avoid tongue biting (particularly while chewing) and monitoring for tongue trauma. Some patients benefit from information regarding neuropathic pain management options if dysesthesia develops during recovery.

Microsurgical Repair and Surgical Considerations

When lingual nerve repair is undertaken—either acutely for intraoperatively identified injuries or secondarily for inadequate recovery after extended observation—microsurgical technique is essential. The procedure typically requires operating microscopy (20-40X magnification), specialized microsurgical instruments including fine forceps and specialized scissors, and microsurgical suture materials (typically 8-0 to 10-0 nylon).

The surgical approach to lingual nerve repair requires careful identification of the injury site and gentle exposure without further trauma. In the oral cavity, approach may be through intraoral incision in the lingual floor of mouth region. In some cases, a combined approach with intraoral and neck incisions provides better exposure. Careful hemostasis and preservation of surrounding structures (lingual artery, hypoglossal nerve) is critical during surgical access.

Nerve repair technique emphasizes exact alignment of proximal and distal stumps with minimal tension. The epineurium (nerve sheath) is identified and carefully sutured with 8-0 to 9-0 nylon, typically creating 2-4 sutures for adequate stabilization. Some surgeons prefer use of fibrin glue or nerve guidance tubes as supplementary fixation. Primary repair aims to restore anatomical continuity without gaps, as gaps exceeding 5-10mm substantially worsen prognosis.

Secondary repair (delayed repair after initial observation) has somewhat better prognosis if undertaken within 2-3 months of injury. At this interval, some preliminary reinnervation may have occurred, and the nerve tissue is still amenable to repair. Beyond 3-6 months, prognosis for sensory recovery decreases substantially, though improvement remains possible. Very delayed repair (>1 year post-injury) rarely produces clinically meaningful sensory recovery.

Conservative Management and Recovery Optimization

For the majority of lingual nerve injuries managed conservatively, patient education and supportive care optimize recovery. Patients should avoid foods requiring precise sensory feedback (hot foods, foods requiring careful mastication) during the recovery period. Modified dietary choices reduce tongue biting risk during the early recovery phase when sensation is completely absent.

Neuropathic medications may be beneficial if dysesthesia (burning sensation) develops during recovery. Gabapentin or pregabalin can reduce neuropathic pain symptoms, improving quality of life during the recovery period. These medications do not accelerate nerve recovery but address symptoms. Dosing typically begins with 300-600mg gabapentin three times daily, titrating upward as needed.

Physical therapy targeting tongue motor control, even though tongue motor function is preserved, may provide psychological benefit by giving patients active participation in recovery. Tongue exercises—touching the teeth, tracing patterns on the palate, gentle stretching—maintain motor tone and may provide subjective improvement in function.

Sensory re-education—deliberate sensory stimulation and attention to sensory input—may facilitate reinnervation. Some evidence suggests that conscious attention to sensory input during healing may enhance neural reorganization, though robust clinical evidence remains limited. Simple strategies including attention to tongue sensation during eating and deliberate sensory stimulation exercises are low-risk and potentially beneficial.

Psychological support becomes important for patients experiencing prolonged sensory disturbance or developing persistent neuropathic pain. Referral to specialists in chronic pain management may be appropriate for patients developing intractable dysesthesia or pain syndromes. Most patients ultimately adapt well even to permanent sensory loss, though the adaptation period may be psychologically challenging.

Medicolegal Considerations and Documentation

Lingual nerve injury represents a well-recognized complication of mandibular surgery, and appropriate informed consent documentation is essential. Pre-operative discussions should address the incidence of temporary and permanent sensory disturbance, recovery timeline, and management options. Documentation of informed consent protects both patient and provider.

Post-injury documentation must include: exact description of injury mechanism, immediate clinical findings, baseline sensory testing results, photographic documentation if appropriate, and communication with the patient regarding findings and prognosis. Serial sensory testing documentation at regular intervals provides objective evidence of recovery progression.

The standard of care includes appropriate counseling regarding avoidance of tongue biting, dietary modifications during early recovery, and discussion of specialist referral if significant recovery delays occur. Failure to provide appropriate counseling or inappropriate dismissal of patient concerns regarding nerve injury can result in medicolegal exposure.

Conclusion

Lingual nerve injury represents a significant complication of oral and maxillofacial surgical procedures, particularly mandibular third molar extraction. Understanding anatomy, recognizing injury mechanisms, and implementing appropriate assessment and management protocols are essential for oral surgeons. The favorable prognosis in the majority of cases—with 85-95% of injured patients experiencing substantial or complete sensory recovery—should be communicated clearly to patients. Careful surgical technique, appropriate risk reduction strategies, and meticulous postoperative management minimize injury incidence while optimizing recovery outcomes in cases where injury occurs.
